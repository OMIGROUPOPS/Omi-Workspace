## SECTION 1: WHERE WE ACTUALLY STAND (READ THIS FIRST)

The bot is shut down. Live trading is not active. Paper mode is running on a config that is explicitly an artifact of broken methodology, not a working baseline.

We know there is a strategy that works. We do not know exactly what it is yet. We have evidence that the structural opportunity exists (e.g., 70.7% double-cash rate at +10c bilateral exits across 458 leader/underdog pairs from April 14 analysis). We have hunches about which directions are strong. But we have not been able to validate them with confidence because the data we have been analyzing on has been compromised in ways we are still discovering.

Why the foundation is not trustworthy:
- Some games recorded entries and exits wrong, which deflated metrics on cells/strategies the operator suspected were strong.
- Multiple bot iterations across months produced layered logs/data with different schemas and different bugs.
- Earlier "rebuild" attempts inherited assumptions from broken methodology without fully realizing it.
- Several measurement methodology errors compounded each other (see Section 5 lessons A, B, F).
- Specific known data corruption: reconcile path overwriting entry_price every 60s (Bug 3, fixed Apr 29); fill quantity under-reporting (POTRYB qty=1 logged, actual=10); missing settlement events when bot resting sell unfilled (Bug 4, in progress).

We cannot reconfigure or redeploy until we trust the foundation. Every config change depends on cell-level data being accurate. Cell-level data depends on entries/exits being recorded accurately. Those records have known errors we have not fully cataloged or corrected.

Wall Street-grade metrics are the bar. Acceptable measurement includes Sharpe, expected value with confidence intervals, drawdown and tail risk, capital efficiency, hit rate times profit at each exit, variance across cells, settlement-loss frequency, Greeks adapted for binary contracts (delta, gamma, theta, vega). Not acceptable: small-N pattern matching, single-metric optimization, per-cell numbers without confidence bounds.

The work right now is foundational, not tactical. We are not optimizing exit_cents. We are not picking which cells to enable. We are rebuilding our ability to trust any per-cell metric at all.

The temptation to start tactical work is the trap. Multiple chats have spun out by skipping this section.

## SECTION 2: WHAT WE KNOW (with current evidence)

### Strategy structure
- The bot is operationally an in-game spike capture mechanism via passively resting sells, not a premarket scalper. Channel 1 (premarket exit) fires 4.7% of the time; Channel 2 (in-game exit) fires 95.3% of the time, median 3.75 hours after entry. The bot has no live game-state intelligence — Channel 2 fires whenever an in-game price move rips through the resting sell, regardless of why.
- Goal: cash both sides of a binary market before settlement when EV supports it. Not every game can double-cash; the strategy edge is positive aggregate EV across all games. April 14 paired analysis (458 leader/underdog pairs) found 70.7% bilateral double-cash rate at +10c bilateral exits — concrete evidence the oscillation edge exists structurally.
- Per-cell calibration is required. Across-the-board uniform exits underperform per-cell tuning. ATP_CHALL both-sides hybrid analysis shows wildly different bilateral capture rates per entry bucket: 5-15c bucket reaches entry times 2 in 76.9% of cases; 80-95c bucket reaches entry times 2 in 0% of cases (but reaches entry+10c in 54.5%). Some cells will be skip-or-single-side, not bilateral.
- Premarket has internal phases: pre-formation (right after Kalshi market open, wild moves), formation period (lines moving as price discovery happens), post-formation/settled premarket (consensus established). Bot uses a 4-hour entry gate to ensure entries are post-formation and FV anchors are reliable. Not perfect — formation timing varies.
- Bot edge thesis (per code) is anchor-relative discount capture. Buys when Kalshi mid is meaningfully below an FV anchor (Scenario C_discount, 97% of fills). Scenarios A_premium and B_take are theoretical in practice.

### Realized P&L history
- Mar 26 to Apr 17 (977 fills, source = live + live_log): minus $1,339.52, mean minus 137c per fill. Bimodal P&L (heavy +500c wins, heavy minus 500c losses, almost nothing in between) confirms bot was riding to settlement, not scalping.
- Apr 24 to Apr 29 (131 JSONL fills, post-bot-retune): 106 resolved at +$17.21 net, mean +16.6c. 58 still resting (unknown outcome). Caveat: if those rest into settlement losses, the post-retune number degrades.
- Leaders carry 84% of dollar bleed despite being 79% of fills. Per-cell concentration: leader_50-54 catastrophic at minus $22.52 per fill, leader_55-59 / 60-64 / 80-84 also bleeding. Worst single fill: leader_80-84 entered at 79c, settled at 1c.

### Bot mechanics
- Bot version: live_v3.py (current). Earlier versions (tennis_v5, arb_executor_v7, swing_ladder) wrote logs/data in different formats now considered legacy.
- Entry logic: Schedule gate, then Intelligence-window gate (4-12hr cap depending on tier), then BBO freshness, then Per-side gates, then Anchor resolution, then Cell lookup, then Scenario classification (A/B/C), then Pre-post race guard, then Place buy.
- Exit logic: After entry_filled, place static resting sell at fill_price + cell_cfg["exit_cents"]. Sell stays in place through pre-match, match start, and the match itself. NEVER cancelled or repriced based on game progression. Position closes when sell fills OR market settles.
- Three scenarios: A_premium (kalshi above anchor, only within 1h of match), B_take (kalshi 8c+ below anchor, take the ask), C_discount (kalshi at-or-slightly-below anchor, post maker). 97% of historical fills are C_discount.
- No live game-state ingestion. Bot has no awareness of score, sets, breaks. Channel 2 capture is purely passive.
- Apr 24 retune disabled 14 cells, retuned 8 exits, made 12 code changes simultaneously. Cannot isolate which intervention drove subsequent improvement.

## SECTION 3: HUNCHES WORTH PROTECTING

These are operator intuitions that may have been deflated by suspect data and may be stronger than current measurements suggest. Treat with care; do not dismiss without re-testing on cleaned data.

- Specific cells the operator suspected were strong have shown weak metrics. The underlying entry/exit records in those cells may have been recorded wrong. Worth re-measuring on cleaned data.
- Strategy thesis evolution suggests real underlying intuition. Cross-platform arb v1 (now retired), then Pendulum paired vol, then STB/Pendulum directional v3, then V4 game-state. The operator has been iterating on a real underlying intuition that has not been cleanly captured in any single config.
- Two-channel exit is an unutilized asset. Operator explicitly stated: having both Channel 1 (premarket) and Channel 2 (in-game) available on the same resting sell is an advantage. Most prior analyses focused only on Channel 1; Channel 2 is where 95% of P&L lives.
- Cell inversion is structural. Every market has 2 cells (one per player). The two cells of a game are inverted (sum to ~$1). This is a property of the data we want to use for cross-check and bilateral capture analysis. Not the strategy itself, but a structural feature the strategy can exploit.

## SECTION 5: LESSONS

Lessons are categorized A through G. New lessons get added at the end of their category with the next index. Lessons that are superseded get a SUPERSEDED tag and a pointer to the replacement; never deleted.

### Category A — Measurement methodology

A1. Settlement-inclusive EV double-counts wins not collected.
A2. Aggregate "pair completion %" varies per cell — never report it as a single number.
A3. DCA sweep ROI had time-ordering flaw — counted "max reached target" without enforcing it happened after DCA trigger.
A4. Uniform breakeven math across heterogeneous cells is wrong; per-cell specific math required.
A5. DCA trigger conditions create selection bias on scenarios sampled.
A6. Strategy layers must be analyzed harmonized, not independent.
A7. Match data resolution to question time scale (DCA fires on second-scale moves; 5-min snapshots miss them).
A8. Blended metrics that mix mechanically-different subsamples (winner vs loser scalps) are dangerous. Loser-side scalp economics is the variable that matters most.
A9. Exit target optimization must be scalp-achievable constrained (entry + exit_cents at most 95c).
A10. Cell classification can disagree at fill time vs at price-bucket time.
A11. Cell mismatches at boundaries (FV-cell vs fill-cell) contaminate per-cell numbers. Verify per-cell N is real.
A12. Sub-cell statistical power may be inadequate. Wider groupings can reveal signal hidden in noisy 5c partitions.
A13. Optimization granularity matters. 5c grid steps miss 2-3c precision improvements.
A14. "Entry price" must be defined relative to bot operational reality, not abstract "earliest trade."
A15. Ground truth from actual bot operations beats simulated entry prices from external data sources.
A16. Maker fills are not symmetric across market sides — verify the bot ACTUALLY trades each cell as designed before trusting cell economics.
A17. FV anchor freshness is necessary but not sufficient. FV accuracy (systematic alignment with sharp consensus) is a separate dimension.
A18. The strategy is not just "buy mispriced binaries" — it is "capture spread between retail-influenced Kalshi consensus and aggregated sharp-book consensus."
A19. The cell label only matches reality if the price used for binning is the actual price at our entry window. first_price from historical_events is the first market trade — possibly hours before our entry — not the premarket BBO at the time we would enter.
A20. Data tiers are not equivalent. Pre-March data is summary-level only; Apr 18+ is tick-level with 5-deep orderbook. Treat A-tier as primary; older tiers as supplementary.
A21. Wall Street-grade metrics are the bar. Acceptable: Sharpe-like risk-adjusted returns, EV with confidence intervals, drawdown, capital efficiency, hit rate times profit, variance across cells, settlement-loss frequency, Greeks adapted for binary contracts. Not acceptable: small-N pattern matching, single-metric optimization, per-cell numbers without confidence bounds.
A22. The measurement universe is not bid/ask/mid/spread. Real edge analysis requires: volume and trade flow (aggressor side, VWAP, volume profile), order book dynamics (iceberg detection, fade vs absorb, depth trajectory), realized volatility and autocorrelation, the Greeks (delta/gamma/theta/vega) properly computed, paired-cell lead-lag and slippage, calendar and contextual variables (time of day, tournament stage, surface, format), order flow microstructure (effective spread, realized spread, market impact). Defaulting to top-of-book bid/ask/spread analyses leaves most signal on the table. When designing measurement, ask: "what dimension of this data have we NOT touched yet?"

A23. Data sources are pulled from but not fully extracted. A-tier CSVs have 5-deep orderbook depth with sizes; we have been using only level 1. tennis.db has 7 underexplored tables (book_prices, kalshi_price_snapshots, live_scores, bookmaker_odds, betexplorer_staging, dca_truth, edge_scores) whose schemas we have not inventoried. The analysis/trades/ directory has 2.75M trade records we have barely touched. Before designing analysis, inventory what fields exist in each source, not just what we have been using.
A24. The variable inventory per data tier is the foundation of analysis design. Every analysis can only access the variables present in its source. We have systematically used 6 of 27 columns from A-tier, ignored min/last/total_trades/timestamps in C-tier, and never inventoried 7 underexplored tables in tennis.db (book_prices, kalshi_price_snapshots, live_scores, bookmaker_odds, betexplorer_staging, dca_truth, edge_scores). Before designing analysis at any depth, enumerate variables available at each tier. Analysis questions that seemed impossible may be answerable from data we already have, and questions we have been answering at depth-1 may be answerable at depth-3+ from the same source.
A25. live_scores has only final set-score outcomes, not in-match state. The columns p1_sets, p2_sets, p1_games, p2_games SUGGEST per-set and per-game tracking, but in samples p1_games/p2_games are empty strings and status is 'finished'. live_scores is insufficient for game-event-level Channel 2 attribution. To attribute Channel 2 captures to specific game events, we would need to source live in-match state from elsewhere (Kalshi live_data API, external scrapers like ESPN). Schema-promised capability does not equal data-populated reality.

A26. Trade-level data with taker_side is already collected in analysis/trades/ CSVs. Schema: ts_et, ticker, price, count, taker_side. 1,693 files, ~2.75M trade records, Apr 19+. Aggressor-side, VWAP, volume profile, and buying-pressure-vs-selling-pressure questions (depth-3 and depth-4 microstructure) are answerable from already-collected data, not requiring new collection.
A27. [PARTIALLY SUPERSEDED — see A29] Depth-1 and Depth-2 analyses exist in multiple revisions without canonical source-of-truth tracking. Per the depth-inventory probe: 8+ per-cell distribution files (exit_sweep_grid, exit_sweep_curves, optimal_exits, baseline_econ, bootstrap_ci_results, rebuilt_scorecard, post_retune_economics, per_cell_real_economics) and 5+ trajectory/bias files (entry_price_bias, entry_price_bias.run1, bias_by_cell_from_matches, swing_script outputs, channel_decomp). Multiple files measure overlapping concepts. Before any new Depth-1 or Depth-2 analysis: identify which prior analysis is canonical, or designate a canonical one explicitly. Do not stack new analysis on fragmented prior work.
A28. Bias measurement requires a tier-appropriate baseline. Three tier-specific implementations are required for full coverage of the operation period. Pre-Mar-20: C-tier limited; bias measurement from historical_events.first_price only, no tick-level baseline available. Mar 20 - Apr 17: B-tier bbo_log_v4 baseline; entry_price_bias.csv is canonical (per F22). Apr 18+: A-tier premarket_ticks 27-column baseline; NO bias measurement exists yet, must be built. Same conceptual measurement, three tier-specific implementations required. Treating a single bias file as universally canonical is wrong; the file's tier-coverage window must match the analysis window.
A29. Per-question canonical designation pattern. When multiple analysis files appear to overlap, check whether they answer overlapping questions or different questions on overlapping data. Eight scorecard files initially read as fragmentation per A27 turned out to be 8 distinct measurements: rebuilt_scorecard.csv (mechanism classification), optimal_exits.csv (exit-cents optimization), baseline_econ.csv (currently-active-config baseline), bootstrap_ci_results.csv (CI validation subset), post_retune_economics.csv (post-retune realized P&L), per_cell_real_economics.csv (operational realized P&L), comparison_real_vs_analysis.csv (predicted-vs-realized diagnostic), pnl_by_cell_config.csv (per-config-version P&L). Cell-coverage overlap between them is structural (each scope is a justified subset of the universe). Per-question canonical-designation table is the correct artifact, not pick-one-and-retire-others. Apply this lens before declaring fragmentation: if N files answer M questions where N=M, they are not fragmented.
A30. Server-side fill history is the canonical source for operational bot questions. /tmp/kalshi_fills_history.json (producer: /tmp/fills_history_pull.py) holds 7,489 server-side fills covering Mar 1 - Apr 29 (re-runnable to refresh). Per-fill schema: action (buy/sell), count_fp (actual executed quantity), created_time (UTC ISO Z), fee_cost, fill_id, is_taker (boolean), market_ticker, no_price_dollars, yes_price_dollars, order_id, side (yes/no), subaccount_number, ticker, trade_id, ts (Unix epoch). Local logs (live_v3 JSONL, tennis_v5.log) remain useful for non-fill events like cell_match decisions and bot-side reasoning, but for ground truth on what executed, kalshi_fills_history.json is canonical.

Future cell-definition work should use volume as a primary partition axis, not just as a sample-quality filter. Per A22 — the variable inventory contained volume_24h, total_trades, and other measures we had been treating as filters rather than predictors.

Cross-reference D6 (LESSONS as durable memory), D9 (confident characterization without verification produces silent misinformation).

A35. Volume per minute is the cleanest match-start anchor available in G9. Visual inspection of KXATPMATCH-25JUN18RUNMCD-RUN (Holger Rune R16 ATP London, Jun 18 2025): pre-match volume is at-or-near zero from market open until ~14:30 ET; at 14:30 ET volume_fp transitions to a sustained non-zero regime that does not reverse until settlement. Trade-rate per minute is a parallel/equivalent signal (sparse pre-match, dense in-match). Price-volatility signals (yes_ask range expansion, spread widening) track but lag volume slightly because price moves require enough trades to move the book whereas volume jumps the instant trades arrive. The candle-volume signal is the basis for porting match_facts_v3 detection logic to G9 universe (the existing v3 producer used B-tier BBO volatility-jump detection, which only covers 2,714 markets in the Mar 20 - Apr 17 window; G9 has 20,110 markets and needs its own detector). Detection rule for v4 producer: match-start = first candle in a sustained run of N>=K consecutive non-zero-volume candles, with smoothing for premarket micro-trades. Validation gate: hand-check against ESPN match times for a sample of 20-50 markets before trusting the signal at scale.
A36. YES/NO fold check empirically confirms cell distributional symmetry across the 50c axis. T21 Phase 2 Check 6 paired 57 mirror cells across (entry_band, regime, spread, volume, category) — e.g., YES=[20,30] paired with YES=[70,80] holding other dimensions constant. Median fold diff between low-band bounce_30min_p75 and high-band drawdown_30min_p75 is +0.01c with std 0.03c. The bounce distribution of YES-underdog cells matches the drawdown distribution of YES-favorite cells almost exactly. Implication: YES-only Layer A producer (build_layer_a_v1.py reads only yes_bid_close and yes_ask_close per probe) captures both YES-underdog and YES-favorite dynamics symmetrically, and NO-side distributional questions can be answered from YES-side cells via the fold without doubling the cell count. Caveat: this validates DISTRIBUTIONAL symmetry only. Per-event paired moment questions (U8 inverse-cell cross-check, E18 bilateral capture) require a different artifact that preserves event_ticker — see B19 + ROADMAP F13/G12. Cross-reference: B16 (Layer A scope), B17 (one-sided book filter), B19 (pairing-key gap), G18 (BBO snapshot semantics).
A37. Strict entry rules trade coverage for honesty, and the cost must be measured before the rule is locked. Rung 0 v0.1 spec defined T-20m entry as "mid_close at the per-minute foundation row at T-20m, with nearest-row fallback." Decision 3 probe measured 89.48% cell-assignment coverage under that rule. Operator sharpened v1.0 to "real trade on the trade tape within ±2 min of T-20m" — no theoretical-mid fallback. Phase 3 actual coverage under the trade-tape rule: 71.7% (14,054 emitted, 5,560 dropouts on 19,614 binary-outcome tickers). The dominant new dropout bucket is `no_trade_near_t20m` at 3,368 tickers (17.2% of corpus) — markets that exist in the foundation but had no trade printed within ±2 min of their T-20m timestamp. Coverage cost of the stricter rule: ~17.8 percentage points. The rule is correct (theoretical mid was misleading; real trade is honest), but the cost has to be visible at the spec layer, not discovered post-build. Future entry-anchor variants (T-1h, T-2h, formation-relative, condition-driven) will face the same coverage-vs-honesty tradeoff at their own anchor points; the trade-tape coverage rate at each candidate anchor should be probed before committing to the rule. Cross-reference E32 (locked cell/exit model), Decision 3 probe at /tmp/decision3_t20m_anchor_probe.json (89.48% under fallback rule; 71.7% actual under strict rule).
A38. Peak-to-settlement saturates for winners; peak-before-first-extreme-touch is the operationally meaningful version. The locked cell/exit model has no stop and rides positions to "first touch of 99c or 1c" as the settlement event. A naive "peak bid from entry to settlement_ts" measurement therefore picks up the post-resolution pin (every winning N has peak_bid ≈ 99c trivially) and inflates winners' bounce metrics with a number that doesn't represent an exit a maker could have actually hit while the outcome was still uncertain. Rung 0 v1.1 amendment resolved this by emitting both: `peak_bid_price_full` (full window through settlement_ts — saturates near 99c for winners, useful as a diagnostic comparator) AND `peak_bid_price_pre_resolution` (window terminates at first_extreme_touch_ts - 1 microsecond — the peak before the outcome is structurally revealed). The headline ranking metric is `peak_bid_bounce_pre_resolution`. The dual-peak design is generalizable: any future strategy that measures "best exit you could have hit" on an outcome-pinned market must distinguish before-the-answer-is-known from after-the-answer-is-known, and the load-bearing column is the former. The spec carries this as an emit-both pattern with the operational answer named explicitly. Cross-reference E32 (locked cell/exit model), Rung 0 spec v1.1 commit abad03b5 Section 3.1, F33 (depth-chain gap — separate constraint, not related).
A39. Absolute cents and ROI are structurally different ranking metrics — never use one as a proxy for the other across bands of different entry prices. A +5c bounce on a 25c entry is a +20% ROI. A +5c bounce on a 75c entry is a +6.7% ROI. Three times the percent return for the same absolute cent move. Any cross-band comparison that ranks by absolute cents alone (e.g., "this cell averages 8c bounce vs that cell averages 8c bounce — they're equivalent") is wrong: the cheaper-entry cell is structurally better per dollar of capital deployed. The geometric corollary is independent: a 25c contract has +74c of available room to 99c whereas a 75c contract has only +24c — the ceiling itself is asymmetric, separate from the per-cent-of-entry math. Therefore: any producer or analysis that ranks cells by bounce magnitude MUST emit both absolute cents AND ROI (= bounce / entry_price) as separate columns; downstream consumers pick the metric appropriate to their decision (absolute cents for capital-deployment / per-contract throughput questions; ROI for percent-return / risk-adjusted-return / cross-band-comparison questions). This is not a thesis to be tested — it is basic arithmetic that should already be reflected in every ranking surface. Surfaced repeatedly by operator across sessions ("hundreds of times"); not codified until now (2026-05-15) because the principle was implicit in G1 (cost-basis ROI), E3 (bleed magnitude as ROI), exit_sweep_curves.csv (per-cell ROI grid), and the V3 paired analysis (Combined_ROI%) — implicit usage in code is not the same as codified doctrine in LESSONS, and the gap allowed Rung 1 metric design to be drafted with absolute-cents-only ranking until operator pushback caught it. Cross-reference G1 (ROI on cost basis), E3 (bleed cells require magnitude-aware analysis), A21 (Wall Street-grade metrics: ROI with CIs).

### Category B — Statistical confidence

B1. N must be statistically meaningful before drawing conclusions. N=8 is variance, not signal.
B2. Two days of live data is confirmation, not evaluation. Historical N=100+ evaluates a config.
B3. Do not conflate evaluation with confirmation. Historical evaluates, live confirms.
B4. CIs computed on per-trade variance vs aggregate-mean variance are different things. Verify which.
B5. Std deviations greater than 100% suggest asymmetric distributions where symmetric CI calculations misrepresent reality.
B6. CIs that are tight on small N can be artifacts of low-variance outcomes (e.g., 5 of 5 same outcome) — not real edge.
B7. Optimization results at the maximum tested value are likely artifacts of testing range, not real optima.
B8. Results that "look too clean" (Sharpe 1.12 across 28 days, 100% scalp WR cells) are suspicious.
B9. "X% improvement" claims without baseline confidence intervals mean nothing.
B10. Settlement events being counted as "scalps" via wide exit targets — verify any optimal exit greater than entry+25c is not a settlement-ride artifact.
B11. "Discrepancies" between numbers cited from different sources may be level-of-aggregation differences, not measurement errors. The +21-37c bias number (operator memory) vs 10.6c (canonical file max) was reconcilable as the same data at different aggregation levels: per-ticker raw values vs per-cell aggregate medians. Before treating a discrepancy as a contradiction or fragmentation: check whether the two cited numbers operate at the same aggregation level (per-fill vs per-ticker vs per-cell vs per-category). When they don't match in granularity, the discrepancy may be illusory.
B12. Derived columns may be redundant flags of categorical columns. The uncalibrated boolean column in rebuilt_scorecard.csv is 1:1 with mechanism=UNCALIBRATED — perfect partition, no information added. Reading "30 cells with uncalibrated=True" and "30 cells with mechanism=UNCALIBRATED" as separate findings would be double-counting the same fact. Before treating two derived statistics as independent signals, verify they are not just two views of the same underlying column.
B13. Threshold-based metrics on bounded variables produce ceiling artifacts. Bilateral capture at +Xc requires both sides first prices to be in [X+1, 99-X]. Matches outside that range cannot mathematically achieve the threshold regardless of any other dynamic. U4 Phase 2 stratified by skew_magnitude showed bilateral capture cliff from 49-59% at skew 0-34 down to 0% at skew 35+, but this cliff is partially the ceiling math (heavy side at 85-89c cannot bounce 15c without exceeding 99c) not a measurement of edge or capturability. Use skew-relative thresholds (capture as fraction of available margin) or restrict to skew bins where the ceiling does not bind. Any future analysis with bounded outcome variables (binary contracts, capped P&L, threshold-based hit rates) must check the ceiling math before interpreting threshold cliffs as edge findings.
B14. Match-level aggregate columns conflate distinct time-window opportunities. The historical_events columns first_price, min_price, max_price, last_price are computed across the entire match window without segmentation. Premarket scalping (entry pre-commence, exit pre-commence) and in-match scalping (entry post-commence, exit post-commence) are structurally different strategies with different risk profiles: premarket has no game-state risk but limited window, in-match has game-state risk but more frequent oscillations driven by point-by-point events. Any analysis on match-level aggregates without time-window decomposition is measuring the union of both opportunities, not either component. U4 Phase 1+2 inherited this conflation; the 76.4% high-volume bilateral capture is a unioned figure. The 70.7% anchor cited in E18 likely has the same issue but original subset criteria are undocumented. C-tier (historical_events) does not support time-window decomposition; B-tier (bbo_log_v4 with commence_time filter, Mar 20 - Apr 17) and A-tier (premarket_ticks for premarket-only, Apr 18+) do. Future bilateral and bounce analyses must decompose by time window to be strategy-actionable.

B15. Every variable in this domain is a flowing time series - price, spread, depth, volume, skew, implied probability. There is no static snapshot of a market except settlement. A bot decides at one moment with the state observed at that moment. Per-match aggregates (first/min/max/last across the entire match window) collapse the temporal structure that drives entry decisions; "1000+ matches achieve 76.4% bilateral capture" averaged across all matches that *eventually* hit 1000 trades regardless of how few trades had occurred at the moment the bot would have entered. The unit of analysis must match the unit of decision. Stratify on values-at-decision-time, not whole-match-aggregates.

B16. Bounce / exit / returns are three separate analysis layers; conflating them breaks reasoning about each. **Layer A (pure bounce per cell)** is a property of the market - measure forward bounce distribution per cell from per-moment data, no exit logic, no fill assumptions, no P&L. **Layer B (exit policy optimization)** is a property of strategy - given Layer A bounce distribution, what exit policy maximizes expected capture. **Layer C (realized returns)** is Layer A + Layer B + fees + slippage + fill probability + capital utilization. Prior cell-economics work (corrected_cell_economics.py and scorecard family) collapsed all three into one calculation, which is why per-cell metrics couldnt be reasoned about cleanly - every parameter change moved multiple things at once. All three layers must stay in separate scripts; never cross-conflate. Validates by showing each layer can be re-run with different parameters without recomputing the others (changing exit policy in B should not require re-running A bounce measurement).

B17. One-sided book is detectable from bid-ask asymmetry; when present, the spread is a lie. The pre-12:30 segment of KXATPMATCH-25JUN18RUNMCD-RUN shows yes_bid dropping to ~$0.05 while yes_ask and last-trade remain stable at ~$0.80. The "spread" reads as 75 cents but the market is functionally one-sided: there is no real two-way book, only a stale or absent bid quote against a live ask + trade tape. Any analysis using mid-price or spread as features must filter out one-sided-book regimes; otherwise mid is a meaningless midpoint between a real ask and a phantom bid, and spread overstates true execution friction by ~10x. Detection: bid drops more than N cents (preliminary threshold N=20-30) while yes_ask + last_trade stay within tick-noise of each other and within K cents of recent trade prices. Equivalent inverse pattern (ask spike-up while bid + trades stable) also qualifies. This is distinct from genuine wide-spread markets where both sides are real but illiquid; the discriminator is whether the trade tape continues to print near one side or the mid.
B18. Layer A/B/C separation must hold under "what makes money" pressure — especially under that pressure. The Layer A/B/C separation per B16 is the discipline that prevents prior cell-economics work from re-breaking. When operator asks a money-question and chat is mid-Layer-A, the temptation is to compress Layer A + Layer B + Layer C into a single response to "answer the money question." That compression is exactly what broke previous cell-economics work and required the entire foundational rebuild. The right response to "what makes money" while at Layer A is not to invent a shortcut Layer C — it is to state what Layer A produces, what Layer B will produce, what Layer C will produce, and acknowledge that Layer A alone does not answer the money question. Stating the layered framing IS the answer; proposing a compression is the failure mode. The discipline applies most when the pressure to skip is highest. Cross-reference B16 (the layer separation), C27 (analytical-foundation discipline / no shortcuts).
B19. Cell-aggregate analysis discards per-event pairing structure that source data preserves trivially. cell_stats.parquet (T29 output) has no event_ticker column — cells are aggregates across many markets keyed on (regime, entry_band, spread, volume, category). The pairing convention is trivial at source: event_ticker = ticker.rsplit("-", 1)[0] strips the side suffix (e.g., KXATPMATCH-25JUN18RUNMCD-RUN and KXATPMATCH-25JUN18RUNMCD-MCD share event_ticker KXATPMATCH-25JUN18RUNMCD). Layer A v1 design choice (cell-aggregate, no event preservation) is consistent with B16 (Layer A = property of the market, not strategy) but creates a real gap for downstream questions that need per-moment pairing: U8 inverse-cell cross-check (does cell X bouncing +5c correlate with inverse cell dipping ~5c at same moment?), E18 bilateral capture (did both sides reach +10c above first-observed price?), and any future game-state attribution work. Per-event analyses require a different artifact at moment-grain with event_ticker preserved (see ROADMAP G12). Cross-reference: B16 (Layer A scope), A36 (distributional fold validates aggregate symmetry).
B20. Hypothesis design for coherence checks must specify expected shape, not just direction. T21 Phase 2 Check 1 tested "leader-price asymptote": forward-bounce magnitude declines monotonically as entry_band rises toward 100c. The data showed an inverted-U: mid-priced cells (20-50c) have the largest median bounce_30min_p75 (~0.13), endpoints both decline (low-band 0.06, high-band 0.05). Spearman corr was -0.08, p=0.38 — not the strong negative monotonic correlation predicted. The data is correct (mid-priced markets have most outcome uncertainty and thus most bounce-headroom in either direction); the hypothesis specified the wrong shape. Lesson: when designing structural-property tests, sketch the expected curve shape (asymptote, inverted-U, monotonic, bell-curve, sigmoid) BEFORE drafting the statistical test. Otherwise an inconclusive result cannot distinguish "data is wrong" from "hypothesis was wrong" without a follow-up probe. Mitigation: a coherence check spec should include a 1-line shape prediction ("we expect an inverted-U centered on 50c with declining tails") as well as the direction. Cross-reference: B14 (decomposition required for strategy-actionability), C27 (analytical foundation discipline).


B21. **MFE vs endpoint-capture: Layer A bounce_Xmin_mean and Layer B time_stop capture_mean measure structurally different things; do not assume they trend together with horizon.** Layer A's bounce_Xmin_mean is the *maximum favorable excursion* within an X-minute window — by definition monotone non-decreasing with window length (you can only reach more peaks with more time). Layer B's time_stop policies capture the *endpoint* bid at minute t+horizon — in mean-reverting markets, the endpoint typically peaks early (at the bounce maximum) then reverts toward fair value at long horizons. Empirical evidence from T31c first run on Layer B v1 outputs (commit 28e8ab7) on Layer A v1 cell_stats (commit 1398c39): of 531 positive-bounce cells in Layer A, 97% show monotone increasing bounce_mean with horizon (median Spearman +0.997 across {5min, 15min, 30min, 60min} windows), confirming MFE structure. Of 354 same cells joined into Layer B time-stop policies, only 38.7% show positive Spearman between time_stop horizon and capture_mean (median rho -0.321 across {1min, ..., 240min}). Both findings are correct; they answer different questions. Implications: (a) time-stop alone is dominated by limit policies in mean-reverting markets, since limit policies *do* capture MFE; (b) Layer C / G11 P&L modeling should emphasize limit-bearing policy classes (limit, limit_time_stop, limit_trailing) — pure time_stop has limited strategic value at long horizons; (c) future spec wording involving "horizon" must specify whether the metric is MFE-style (peak within window) or endpoint-style (price at horizon end), because they trend opposite directions. Cross-reference: B16 (Layer A/B/C separation; A is bounce property, B is strategy property given A, C is realized economics; the metric distinction lives at the A->B handoff), B20 (hypothesis-shape mismatch — assumed monotone where the world has different shape; B21 is a specific instance: the spec assumed time_stop would mirror MFE, but it mirrors endpoint), F30 (in_match volume_intensity collapse — distinct issue, but same family of "spec-prediction-vs-empirical-shape" findings).


B22. **Cells are anchored at fill, not predicted in advance — cell-level analysis is retrospective, not prospective.** Markets drift across cells during premarket as price discovery happens (a market may pass through 35¢, 42¢, 50¢, 48¢ over hours, traversing multiple entry_band buckets in a single match's lifecycle). Whichever (channel, category, entry_band, spread_band, volume_intensity) the market is in *when our maker post fills* becomes our cell — the market picks the cell for us by deciding when our limit hits, we don't pick the cell prospectively. Implications: (a) backtest simulations and stress tests must NOT assume "we entered at the closing premarket price" or any other single point — they must consider the full distribution of fill-points consistent with the maker post strategy; (b) cell-level statistics (Layer A bounce, Layer B capture, Layer C realized economics) are *retrospective conditional* on having filled in that cell, not prospective predictions of where we'd land; (c) optimal strategy is therefore not "predict the right cell to target" but "post passive maker bids at attractive prices and let the market's drift fill us into whichever cell happens to be reachable." Cross-reference E20 (per-cell treatment), E18 (bilateral capture), B23 (bilateral mechanism that follows from this anchoring property), B16 (Layer A/B/C separation discipline).

B23. **Bilateral capture mechanism in operational terms (the strategy E18 names abstractly).** Binary tennis markets settle YES=$1 (player A wins) or NO=$1 (player A loses). Posting a maker buy on YES at price P_y AND a maker buy on NO at price P_n captures both sides bilaterally if both posts fill before settlement. Cost basis: P_y + P_n. Settlement guarantees one of the two contracts pays $1 (whichever side wins). **Profit per pair = $1 - (P_y + P_n).** Profitable iff P_y + P_n < $1. Concrete example from Session 8 conversation: post YES at 30¢ AND NO at 60¢. Total cost basis 90¢. Settlement guarantees $1 payout regardless of match outcome. Net +10¢ per contract pair, **no match-outcome risk**. Feasibility per match: both posts must fill during premarket drift, which requires premarket trajectory width ≥ 10¢ minimum (NO at 60¢ ↔ YES at 40¢; YES has to touch BOTH 30¢ and 40¢ within premarket window). Wider trajectory = more feasibility margin. Bot's existing "ladder" pattern works this mechanism — posting maker bids at multiple price levels simultaneously across both YES and NO orderbooks, riding the depth-chain to ladder into bilateral positions. Operator (Session 8): "i've seen us have both sides and positions cash premarket. like +10cs sometimes its hidden value." Cross-reference E18 (bilateral capture goal at 70.7% double-cash rate at +10c), E21 (bilateral rate is per-cell, deep-skew cells likely zero), B22 (cell is anchored at fill, supports bilateral framing), B24 (YES vs NO orderbook asymmetry in execution).

B24. **YES and NO orderbooks for the same match are NOT equivalent in execution surface, even though their payoffs are mathematically inverse.** Buying NO on player A pays $1 if A loses; buying YES on player B (where B is A's opponent) also pays $1 if A loses. Same outcome dependence, same payoff per contract. **But the orderbooks are separate.** Different liquidity, different depth, different spread, different volume profile, different OI accumulation. Generally: cheap side (the underdog) tends to attract more participants on the YES orderbook than the favorite's YES orderbook does, because longshot speculation is bid-heavy on the underdog. Strategy implication: ladder on whichever side has deeper book, regardless of which directional payoff is mathematically equivalent. C19. Kalshi market-lifecycle timestamps (expected_expiration_time, open_time, close_time, settlement_ts) do not track actual match start. C20. Multi-source data joins require explicit grain checks before the join runs. event-grain (1 row per match in historical_events) vs market-grain (2 rows per match in match_facts and bbo_log) is silent - joins succeed structurally and produce empty or wrong results. Session 5 hit this twice: 0/20 historical_events vs match_facts_full overlap looked like format mismatch but was grain mismatch; same root cause for 53.7% bbo_log skip rate later. TAXONOMY should track grain per column (event vs market vs tick vs aggregate), not just per table.

C21. A-tier features cannot be retroactively synthesized from B-tier without an overlap-window calibration step. Synthesis (e.g., inferring depth from BBO recovery time, inferring volume rate from BBO update rate) requires ground-truth comparison against actual A-tier data on the overlap window with quantified error bounds. Without calibration, synthesized signals are fiction wearing a confidence interval. Process: for each candidate synthesized signal, define the model linking B-tier observable to A-tier ground truth, calibrate on the overlap, validate generalization to the B-tier-only window, report measured error. Dont apply uncalibrated synthesis retroactively.

C22. Shell-conditional return code checks must check the right command. `python3 -c "import X" 2>&1 | head -3` followed by `if [ $? -ne 0 ]` checks `head`s exit code (always 0), not pythons. Caused a self-inflicted Stage 1 launch failure Session 5 - auto-install fallback never triggered because the conditional was wired wrong. Reliable patterns: `python3 -c "import X" || pip install X --break-system-packages` (no pipe in between) or capture exit code immediately: `python3 -c "import X"; rc=$?` before any subsequent pipe.

C23. Derive epoch timestamp constants from ISO strings inline at write-time, never from memory or arithmetic. Session 5 G9 producer launched with CUTOFF_TS = 1772582400 (decodes to Mar 4 2026 UTC) when the intended value was Mar 2 2026 UTC = 1772409600. Off by exactly 2 days. The bug shipped to production despite a comment claiming the constant was correct. Cause: epoch was hardcoded as a number with the ISO date in a comment, treated as verified, and never re-derived from the ISO string. Caught after 30 seconds of producer runtime - minimal cost - but in a longer-running pipeline this would have silently dropped 2 days of data at the boundary between historical and live tiers, exactly where data discontinuities are hardest to surface in downstream analysis. Discipline: any timestamp constant in a producer or pipeline must be derived inline from the ISO string in code that runs each time, e.g., `CUTOFF_TS = int(datetime(2026, 3, 2, 0, 0, 0, tzinfo=timezone.utc).timestamp())`. Never paste a hardcoded epoch number with the date in a comment - the comment lies easily, the derivation cannot.

C24. Multi-endpoint-family API discipline. Don't declare an API-coverage horizon based on testing one endpoint family. Probe API surface broadly before concluding "this data isn't available." Session 5 example: Stage 0 Probe 2 declared trade-tape data unavailable after testing one Kalshi endpoint, when /historical/trades + /historical/markets/{ticker}/candlesticks worked retroactively to mid-2025 (G9 dataset later confirmed full archive availability). Discipline: before declaring an API can't deliver X, enumerate the endpoint families relevant to X (e.g., /markets, /events, /series, /historical/*, /portfolio, /exchange), probe each at minimum surface (one call per family with realistic parameters), and document the coverage matrix. Single-endpoint negative results are not coverage conclusions.

C25. Heredoc quote handling: write to local temp file, pipe via stdin, don't fight Bash quoting layers. SSH-piped heredocs containing apostrophes or single quotes (e.g., "Liam's chart", "don't catastrophize") get mangled by the local Bash interpreting the quotes inside the outer 'OUTER_EOF' sentinel before the SSH transmission. Symptom: "unexpected EOF while looking for matching `''" mid-script. Fix pattern: write the script to a local temp file (e.g., C:\Users\omigr\AppData\Local\Temp\phase_X.sh), then ssh root@host 'bash -s' < /path/to/script.sh. The < operator passes the file as stdin to remote bash without local-bash quote interpretation. Surfaced Session 6 Phase 1D-iii (T24 entry referenced "Liam's Jun 2025 ticker chart") and used throughout subsequent Phase 1D and Phase 4 commits.

C26. For destructive or non-idempotent actions, always probe live state before executing — even when the action looks like a re-run of something just sent. T17 producer launch (Session 6 ~19:25 ET): chat sent the launch script, CC ran it (PID 3887519), then chat sent the same launch script again as a duplicate. CC's pre-launch probe checked process state, saw the producer already running with valid in-flight work (10% done, 0 failures, parquet being written), and stopped before re-running. A second launch would have produced two processes writing to the same g9_candles.parquet simultaneously, corrupting it, plus interleaved log writes. The discipline: any action that creates, overwrites, deletes, or launches a long-running job must verify pre-action state regardless of whether it looks like a continuation. Idempotent actions (str_replace with disk-state checks, append-only log writes, read-only probes) don't need this gate; non-idempotent ones (file creation, process launch, schema migrations, git commits with destructive operations like rebase) always do. Same principle family as D15 (disk wins) but applied to running processes instead of file content.

C27. Every analysis carries its foundational-data dependency explicitly; no claim survives the question "verified against what." The discipline: before any analysis runs, its input foundation is verified (probe its row counts, schema, reconstruction-equivalence to source); the producer + verification are commit-pinned; the analysis output's catalog entry references the foundation commit. Downstream analyses carry their own dependency-commit pointer (e.g., Layer B output references "Layer A output at commit X"). When a foundation gets re-run with a fix, downstream entries are flagged for re-run via the dependency chain — no silently stale results. The failure mode this prevents: "I think the data shows..." replacing "this data, verified at this commit, shows..." Operator-stated principle: "we're doing this the right way, no shortcuts." Sequence template for any new analysis: (1) verification probe on input foundation — cheap, gates everything downstream per D11, (2) foundation commit logging artifact checksums + verification result, (3) ANALYSIS_LIBRARY entry registering canonical artifact + validity status + source-of-truth pointer, (4) producer code committed, (5) producer runs, (6) output registered in ANALYSIS_LIBRARY with foundation-commit pointer. Steps 1-3 cannot be skipped because Layer A visuals are functions of the parquet foundation, Layer B exit policies are functions of Layer A output, Layer C realized economics are functions of A+B+fees+slippage. Each layer inherits the integrity of the layer below. Cross-reference: D15 (disk wins), D11 (cheap probes prevent expensive mistakes), F1 (code-config-data triangulation), B16 (Layer A/B/C separation).

C28. Verification probes on large parquets (or any large dataset) must use streaming or filter-pushdown to stay under the VPS RAM cap. T27 v1 was OOM-killed (exit code 137 = SIGKILL on memory pressure) at CHECK 5 when it tried to materialize a 9.5M-row column via pq.read_table() + .to_pylist() — that expanded to >1.5GB Python-object footprint, exceeding 1.9GB available. Same failure-mode family as the B-tier OOM in Session 4. Apply to T17 parquet ops and any future analysis on G9-scale data. Cross-reference: D11 (cheap probes), F1 (code-config-data triangulation).

C29. **Bash `set -e` + `grep` verification gates are brittle. `grep -c` and `grep -q` exit 1 on zero matches, but zero matches is often the desired outcome of a post-edit residue check ("confirm the old text is gone"), and `set -e` interprets the non-zero exit as failure, aborting the script after the edit succeeded.** Surfaced 4 times in Session 7 alone: (a) T31a patch 5 sed-followup probe — `grep -c 'patch'` returned 0, set -e killed the probe script before its tail commands ran, cosmetic but obscured a real probe finding; (b) T31b-β key-fix script — `grep -q "'type':"` (single-quote spec text not on disk because file uses double-quotes), gate aborted before the actual edit which would have worked regardless; (c) patch-8 spec edit script — post-write `grep -c "between limit_c and capture_mean"` returned 0 (the desired outcome — the phrase had just been replaced) but `set -e` interpreted as failure, aborted before commit even though edits were correctly applied; (d) patch-8 recovery script — `git status --short | wc -l` counted 300+ untracked files plus the 2 modified-tracked files, the count failed an `if -ne 2` gate. Mitigations: (1) append `|| true` after greps and similar commands whose zero-match outcome is desired; (2) use `[ "$(grep -c X file)" = "0" ]` with explicit comparison instead of relying on grep's exit code; (3) for git-status counts, scope to specific files via `git status --porcelain path1 path2`; (4) prefer per-edit precondition asserts that block when the *target text isn't there* over post-edit residue asserts that block when the *replaced text is gone* — only the first prevents wrong writes; the second can incorrectly block correct ones. Generalizes: any verification gate where the "expected" exit code may be non-zero must explicitly handle that case under `set -e`. Cross-reference: D15 (chat-side draft drift — separate family but same surface, both involve chat-drafted strings/conditions not matching disk reality); C25 (file-staging via heredoc with sentinel — when this discipline is followed for the actual content, the verification-gate brittleness is the leftover failure mode).


C30. **Schema-probe-depth discipline: a column appearing in a schema is necessary but not sufficient evidence for "this data exists in usable form."** When proposing reconstruction-from-X to fill a perceived "missing" field, the discipline is: probe ALL plausible data sources for the field's presence, semantics, and population reliability BEFORE drafting the reconstruction plan. F31 (Session 8 evening) is the canonical anchor: framed Open Interest as "not currently tracked at the data layer" requiring ~2-3 hours of trade-tape reconstruction. The g9_candles schema had been probed earlier in Session 8 and `open_interest_fp` was visible in the column listing, but the connection "schema has OI column" → "we don't need to reconstruct" was missed. Session 9 morning's 60-second probe (read 5 sample tickers' OI trajectories + cross-check candle_final_oi vs metadata_final_oi) revealed 96.4% population reliability and instantly invalidated the F31 reconstruction plan. **The discipline check before claiming "X is not tracked":** (1) does the schema list a candidate field? (2) is it populated for representative samples (not just first-row of one market)? (3) does its semantics match the trading concept? (4) what is per-tier coverage? All four take seconds-to-minutes to verify; the cost of skipping them is hours of unnecessary reconstruction work + downstream lessons/ROADMAP that have to be amended. Family with C28 (streaming discipline — also probe-first), D11 (cheap probes prevent expensive mistakes), C29 (set -e + grep-c brittleness — the verification gate, applied here is "probe shows the field is populated, not just listed"). Cross-reference: F31 (the lesson amendment that surfaced this), T33 (the ROADMAP item simplified by the same probe).


C31. **Mechanism-coherent hypotheses about alpha are not evidence the alpha exists empirically; first-probe verification is required before any deliverable producer is scoped.** A hypothesis can pass framing-coherence (no-arbitrage on binary markets implies combined trade price ≈ $1.00; deviations are mispricings to harvest), be intuitive from market structure (formation chaos, one-sided takers, locked opposing books), and still produce zero observable events at first operational test. F32's original framing — "trivially computable from g9_trades, direct alpha signal" — was empirically tested at corpus scale by Cat 9 (commit 631e653; ε=$0.01, per-minute close, 33.7M trades / 20K markets / 3.2M minute-bins) and returned zero events. The right epistemic posture on first-probe-zero is not "the hypothesis is wrong" or "the data is incomplete" but **"the claim is downgraded from alpha signal to alpha hypothesis until deeper probes resolve why first contact returned zero."** Three legitimate reasons a mechanism-coherent hypothesis can fail first probe: (a) threshold too strict relative to the natural scale of the effect, (b) resolution too coarse relative to the timescale of the effect, (c) the venue's structural mechanics genuinely don't produce the effect at observable density. All three are empirical questions answered by deeper probes, not by argument. **Operational rule:** when proposing a new alpha-hypothesis lesson or an alpha-hypothesis-derived T-item, the proposal must explicitly name the first-probe operationalization that would test it (threshold + resolution + scope + acceptance criterion). When that probe runs and returns zero, this lesson governs the demotion path before any second probe is committed: amend the source lesson, close any deliverable-producer T-item that depended on it as superseded, canonicalize the negative result in ANALYSIS_LIBRARY Section 4 anchor evidence, and either queue deeper probes as new T-items or accept the venue-level absence as a structural property. The Simons-mode discipline (SIMONS_MODE.md Section 7 rollback question 5) is concentration on verified empirical density, not framing coherence. Family with B20 (specify expected curve shape before drafting test), B21 (do not assume metrics trend together with horizon), C30 (schema-probe-depth — column existence ≠ usable data); distinct from each — B20/B21 govern hypothesis specification, C30 governs data presence verification, C31 governs alpha-mechanism empirical-presence verification. Anchor case: F32 amendment, this commit.

- **C32 — match_start_method=unknown is canonical correct for early-settling matches** *(2026-05-12)*
  When per_minute_universe's match_start detection 4-tier hierarchy (bilateral price discovery, trade density, expected_expiration fallback, unknown fallback) falls through to "unknown" for a ticker, this is not a producer bug — it is the canonical correct disposition for matches that ended earlier than scheduled (walkover, retirement, quick-finish, mid-match cancellation). Probe of 8 such tickers across WTA_CHALL, ATP_MAIN, WTA_MAIN categories showed all had `expected_expiration_ts > settlement_ts`, meaning the match settled before the schedule predicted. Downstream consequence: regime classification falls back to "premarket" for all minutes (no in_match transition); forward-looking labels still compute correctly. Tolerance threshold: up to ~6% of corpus carrying this disposition is acceptable per LESSONS A35.

- **C33 — Optimization regressions are real risk; byte-equiv AND timing-benchmark are joint pass criteria** *(2026-05-12)*
  An "optimization" can be slower than the baseline it replaces. Today's T37 producer journey demonstrated this empirically: the v3 commit (398706e1) introduced numpy vectorization via `searchsorted + bincount` for trade aggregation but retained pandas `groupby + apply` for the `trade_clustering` calculation. The result was 7% slower than the v2 pandas baseline (6.83s → 7.50s per-ticker median), because pandas DataFrame construction overhead exceeded the savings from vectorized bincount at typical ticker sizes (~200-300 trades). The v4 commit (dbdd08ac) delivered the real 39% speedup (7.50s → 4.58s) by eliminating pandas entirely and computing per-bucket std-of-gaps from raw numpy moments. The discipline gate that caught this: byte-equiv content validation AND measurable wall-clock speedup as JOINT pass criteria. Either criterion alone would have shipped a broken optimization. Operational rule: any optimization claim must demonstrate both content equivalence (byte-equiv on stable columns, ≤1e-9 tolerance on floating-point reductions) AND timing improvement (measurable, not theoretical) before being committed. Cheap probes before expensive commitments; 60-min spike with strict budget caught the v3 regression and produced the v4 win that fits Phase 3 into a single overnight window.

- **C34 — Foundation rebuild discipline: contracts before salvage** *(2026-05-13)*  
  In foundation rebuilds, the fastest-looking path ("we already have working code; patch it forward") is often the highest-risk path. The trap is that foundation-coupled decision logic survives refactors because old abstractions feel reusable until their implicit assumptions are traced: routing schemas, position models, and entry/exit lifecycle objects often encode the previous foundation's unit-of-analysis, state model, and decision rules even when wrapped in new modules. The discipline is: **establish the new contracts first** — especially the data-access contract and the new decision/config schema — before porting any salvaged components. Salvage must attach to the new contracts; the new contracts must never be shaped around salvaged legacy structures. Specific application: V5 must not port `LiveV3`'s `cell_lookup()`, `Position`, or entry/DCA/exit semantics under a "refactor later" rationale. Those structures encode the old foundation and will recapture the new architecture if inserted before the new cell-config schema and foundation data layer exist. General rule: on any future foundation rebuild, define the new canonical interfaces first, then port only infrastructure that is genuinely foundation-agnostic.

- **C35 — Merge step memory discipline for kill-resilient producers** *(2026-05-14)*  
  Kill-resilient batched producers correctly avoid holding the full corpus in memory during the expensive ticker loop, but the merge step that consolidates per-batch outputs is a separate memory failure mode that must be designed with the same discipline. T37 Phase 3 demonstrated this: ticker loop completed cleanly across 27.4 hours with 197 batch parquets totaling ~9.5M rows preserved on disk, but the merge step attempted to load all batches simultaneously (via pd.concat or equivalent) and OOM'd on the 1.9GB VPS. The fix is structural: use pyarrow.ParquetFile().iter_batches() + pyarrow.ParquetWriter to stream batches one at a time during merge, keeping peak memory bounded to ~10-30MB regardless of corpus size. Generalize: any producer using per-batch durability + final-merge architecture must apply streaming I/O to BOTH the writer and the merger, not just the writer. The OOM during merge cost no data (kill-resilience protected all 19,614 batch outputs) but cost ~15 minutes of triage and a producer fix commit. Cheap lesson; expensive without this discipline at larger corpus scales.

- **C36 — Candle-source vs trade-tape-source aliasing for the same semantic** *(2026-05-14)*  
  When two columns answer the same question ("what trade activity happened this minute"), they must derive from the same canonical source. T37's producer sourced trade_count_in_minute from the trade tape (g9_trades, np.bincount, zero-filled by construction) but sourced volume_in_minute from the candle aggregate (g9_candles.volume_fp), which is 65% null at source due to Kalshi historical-candles API schema drift. Result: 56% of rows with verified real trades showed volume_in_minute = NULL. The producer already computed the correct trade-tape volume (total_count at line 304) and used it for vwap, but discarded it rather than storing it. Discipline: for any feature answering a trade-activity question, default to the trade tape as the canonical source — it is microsecond-precise and zero-filled by construction. Candle aggregates are a secondary/derived source with their own null conventions and era-based schema drift (see F29). Two columns answering the same semantic question must never be sourced from different inputs with different null conventions. **Corroborating empirical (2026-05-14):** taker_side in g9_trades is 100% populated and exactly {"yes", "no"} (no nulls, no other values across 33.7M rows / 5.98B ct volume). Even on the 1.37M rows where both candle volume_fp > 0 and trade tape > 0 were "known-good", they disagreed on 12.12% — direction always candle > trade tape, integer-contract deltas (typical +1 ct/minute). Likely cause: Kalshi candle aggregate captures settlement / auto-fill / system-generated volume the discrete trade event stream doesn't. Operational rule: the trade tape is canonical for discrete trade activity, even on rows where the candle aggregate is populated and positive. **Zero-size-trade discovery (addendum 2026-05-14):** g9_trades contains 67,191 events with `count_fp = 0` (0.20% of 33.7M rows; zero null, zero negative, only `{yes, no}` taker_side). These events propagate `+1` to any row-counting aggregate (e.g., `np.bincount` without weights) but `0` to any contract-volume-weighted aggregate. If two columns nominally answer the same activity question but one is row-count and the other is weighted sum, they diverge silently on these 67,191 events. Discipline extension: aligning two columns on the same canonical source (the trade tape) is necessary but not sufficient — they must also align on the SAME row-inclusion filter (e.g., `count_fp > 0`). T37 producer commit `4b0c3e45` filters zero-size trades from `trade_count_in_minute` so it aligns with `volume_in_minute` on positive-size events; the strict invariant `(tc>0)⇔(vol>0)` then holds by construction. Anchor: g9_trades-direct backfill at checkpoint 3 sha256 `9fde4b5d…`.

- **C37 — Corpus mutations require a pre-replace validation gate** *(2026-05-14)*  
  Atomic replace (write .new, then os.replace) protects the original file from a crashed or interrupted mutation — but it does NOT protect against atomically swapping in a .new file that completed successfully and is wrong. The g9_trades volume backfill v1 ran to near-completion and wrote a .new file with all 9.33M volume_in_minute values set to zero (an mts minute-bucketing bug collapsed 33.7M trades into 42,870 buckets instead of ~3M+). It was saved from swap-in ONLY by an incidental unrelated crash (a bare log() call) that aborted the script before os.replace. That is luck, not design. The discipline: every corpus mutation must run a hard validation gate against the .new file BEFORE os.replace — row/schema parity, the core semantic invariants the mutation is meant to establish, distribution sanity, and (critically) sanity on intermediate computation artifacts like bucket counts. os.replace fires only on all-gates-pass; any failure keeps the original and preserves the .new for inspection. Atomic replace is necessary but not sufficient — it must be paired with a pre-replace gate. Related: C35 (streaming-merge memory discipline), C4 (destructive-action authorization). **Adjudication addendum (2026-05-14):** Gate failures get adjudicated WITH EVIDENCE (probe the actual failing rows, surface their concrete state from disk), never overridden on a summary. The v3 backfill produced three gate failures — GATE 3 (1,523 rows), V2 (44,783 rows), V3 (27 rows) — that were each tempting to dismiss as "obviously the Option B reach" or "obviously the C36 phenomenon at small scale". Probing the actual rows confirmed: GATE 3's 1,523 = all-zero-size-only minutes (consistent under Option B); V2's 44,783 = mixed-zero+positive-trade minutes (Option B at trade level not minute level); V3's 27 = candles reporting +1 ct on minutes where every discrete trade was zero-size (C36 mechanism at extreme). The probes turned each gate failure into either (a) a benign-by-definition pass with documented evidence, or (b) — if it had been a real bug — a clear halt. The discipline is: when a gate fails, the path forward is evidence-from-disk, not narrative-from-summary. The probe IS the gate; the gate output is only an alarm. Anchor: 27-row V3 probe at sha256 `9fde4b5d…` checkpoint 3.

### Category D — Memory / context discipline


D1. Compaction summaries lose strategic framing while preserving tactical bullets. When operator references "what we concluded," check the actual conversation, not the summary.
D2. Memory bullets describing concepts ("scalping AND inversion cells are BOTH a thing") are not the same as conclusions about which cells fall into which category. The lens is not the verdict.
D3. Memory bullets describing scorecards by counts ("11 confirmed SCALPER_EDGE, 4 bleed, 30 UNCALIBRATED") may be summary-of-summary, not ground truth. Verify against the actual artifact before designing analysis around the asserted shape.
D4. When operator pushes back, do not generate another plausible-sounding framework. Stop. Ask. Re-anchor. Pattern-matched fabrication compounds.
D5. Diligence means thinking through the response shape and what could be missing BEFORE the response lands, not after.
D6. This LESSONS.md is the durable memory. Future chats start by reading this file. Do not reinvent context the file already has.
D7. Schema columns suggest capability; populated content determines reality. A column named p1_games does not mean per-game state is captured if the column is empty in practice. A column named bookmaker_price does not mean sharp consensus is available if 100% of values are NULL. Before designating a table as a strategic source, verify population status (NULL rate, sample distribution, distinct value count) for the columns that matter. The schema is a promise; the data is the truth.

D8. Before drafting any additive change to LESSONS.md or any module file, first verify on-disk state of that file. Drafted-in-chat content is not on-disk content. A commit prompt drafted N turns ago may not have been executed; the user response may have been about a different topic. Verify before designing operations that depend on prior commits having landed. D9. Confident characterization without verification produces silent misinformation that future sessions inherit. The depth-inventory probe casually described /root/Omi-Workspace/tmp/ as a "single-mtime snapshot from Apr 29 17:43" — partially true (one large batch had that mtime) but wholly wrong as characterization (it's a multi-batch git-tracked curated archive with batches across Mar 13-15, Apr 20, Apr 29, and other dates). I wrote that characterization into ANALYSIS_LIBRARY.md uncorrected. Future Claude would have inherited the error. Mitigation: when a probe output contains a structural characterization (this directory is X kind of thing), verify the characterization with a targeted follow-up before it lands in durable docs. Sampling more than one mtime, more than one file, more than one signal. Same class as D5 (think through response shape before it lands), D7 (schema vs population), C2 (always be suspicious of CC output).
D10. After CC renames a drafted lesson via Path 1 rewrite, the on-disk lesson number differs from the originally-drafted number. Future drafts must read on-disk state and number from there, not from chat history of what was drafted. Session 4 had three such off-by-one drift events: drafted A28 landed as A27 (D1/D2 fragmentation lesson), drafted A29 landed as A28 (tier-appropriate bias baseline), drafted A30 landed as A29 (per-question canonical pattern). Each draft was one ahead because the chat-side mental model did not track the rename. Mitigation: before drafting any new numbered lesson, treat the on-disk file as authoritative; either read it via CC pre-flight or query before drafting. Do not infer the next number from chat history. Pre-flight assertions in the write script (asserting "Nxx in doc" and "Nyy not in doc") catch the drift but cost a Path 1 round-trip every time. Better to draft from on-disk state in the first place.

D11. Cheap probes prevent expensive mistakes. The cost asymmetry is enormous - a 1-min `PRAGMA table_info` vs a 9-hour bad-foundation compute run; an 80-second calibration probe vs running Stage 1 with a 13.5-hour-stdev time anchor; a 30-second grep on bbo_log skip rate vs analyzing data we know is incomplete. Session 5 was this lesson playing out repeatedly. The discipline is not "be skeptical of conclusions" - its "be skeptical of inputs." Conclusions that look wrong get questioned naturally; conclusions that look right on broken data are the dangerous ones because they validate the path that produced them. Five recurring failure modes hit during foundational data verification - interrogate every dataset for all five before analysis runs:

D12. Volume-stratified pre-sampling for cost extrapolation. When estimating runtime or cost on a producer that processes heterogeneous units (markets, files, tickers), single-unit timing tests can be 3x or more off if the chosen unit is not representative of the cost-driving variable. Session 5 G9 producer runtime was estimated from one low-volume Challenger market test; the resulting estimate was 3x the actual runtime because high-volume markets dominate the total cost. Discipline: before extrapolating from a sample, identify the cost-driving variable (volume, file size, market activity), bucket the population by that variable, and sample stratified across buckets. Median runtime across stratified samples is a better extrapolation base than mean of one arbitrary sample. Same class as B14/A31 (volume as primary axis) but applied to producer cost estimation.

D13. Stale user-memory must be treated as historical context, not current state. The user-memory tool surfaces a snapshot from prior sessions; it is not a live read of the current operational state. When user-memory and SESSION_HANDOFF disagree on what is current, SESSION_HANDOFF wins because it was written at the end of the most recent session. When SESSION_HANDOFF and on-disk state disagree, disk wins because disk was written by the actual operations being described. Session 6 opening hit this twice: chat treated user-memory's "Mona Lisa config deployed" as live state when SESSION_HANDOFF said the bot was shut down, and treated user-memory's "the foundation was broken" framing as a current crisis when ROADMAP/LESSONS already had it captured-and-disposed. Mitigation: when reading user-memory, prefix every claim mentally with "as of an unknown prior time" until it's verified against SESSION_HANDOFF and disk.

D14. Session-handoff documents need a ground-truth anchor section. A handoff doc that's all narrative without an explicit operational-state anchor section makes the next session reconstruct ground truth from prose, which is slow and error-prone. The ground-truth anchor names: what's deployed (or not), what's in flight, what's blocked, what changed in the last session, what's the immediate next move. Session 6 opening was hindered by the previous handoff being narrative-heavy; the recovery required reading LESSONS + ROADMAP + ANALYSIS_LIBRARY end-to-end to reconstruct what was current. - **F35 — Snapshot-grain deduplication** *(2026-05-12)*
  When orderbook snapshots are persisted with one row per active price level, `snapshot_ts` will appear multiple times — once per populated level in that snapshot. Before iterating snapshots to build reconstruction windows or to run snapshot-boundary validation checks, deduplicate per `(ticker, snapshot_ts)` and operate only on the distinct set of snapshot anchors. Failing to deduplicate silently multiplies iteration work by the number of populated levels and distorts window boundaries by treating one logical snapshot as many. This is not a KDB-specific issue; it is a generic consequence of storing book state in long form. Marriott (2026) surfaced this as an implementation pitfall after pre-parsing snapshots into one-row-per-level form and observing duplicate timestamp iteration during reconstruction.

- **F36 — Grouped timestamp semantics for orderbook deltas** *(2026-05-12)*
  Treat all orderbook deltas sharing identical `(ticker, event_ts)` as one logical update. Apply every delta in the timestamp group to the in-memory book first, then emit the resulting book state once for that timestamp. This preserves the canonical book state when multiple order updates arrive within the same millisecond and prevents per-delta intermediate states from being mistaken for the true book at that moment. The rule is load-bearing for any `get_book_at` primitive and for any per-tick or intra-minute reconstruction derived from the stream. Marriott (2026) makes this explicit in the Section 4.1 reconstruction algorithm, noting that grouped-emit behavior is the correct semantics when multiple updates share a timestamp.

- **F37 — Batch construction over row-by-row append** *(2026-05-12)*
  Row-by-row appends to columnar systems are not a scaling strategy. For high-throughput event producers and reconstruction pipelines, accumulate typed columnar vectors in memory and write once per batch or chunk; only then build the table or Parquet row group. This principle is engine-agnostic: it applies to KDB+, Parquet writers, pandas, Polars, Arrow, and similar columnar systems because repeated row append defeats the locality and amortization advantages those systems are built for. At full-universe Kalshi scale, this is mandatory rather than an optimization. Marriott (2026) reports that vector accumulation followed by one-shot table construction was necessary because table append was materially slower at billions-of-rows scale.

- **F38 — Per-ticker reconstruction for memory safety** *(2026-05-12)*
  Bound memory in reconstruction pipelines by processing one ticker, or at most a small shard of tickers, at a time and flushing output to disk before moving on. Avoid designs that hold multi-ticker full-depth states and accumulated output vectors in memory simultaneously across a large universe, because the failure mode is catastrophic rather than graceful: KDB Community raised a memory stop signal in Marriott's implementation, while in Python the analogous outcome is process slowdown, swapping, or OOM kill. This pattern is independent of language and database choice; it is a structural property of full-depth reconstruction at large scale. Marriott (2026) solved the memory ceiling by reconstructing one market at a time and writing results to disk before continuing.
