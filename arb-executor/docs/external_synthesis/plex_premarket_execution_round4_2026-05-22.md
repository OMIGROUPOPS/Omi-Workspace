## Sub-question A: Both-Sides-Positive Taker Flow During Volatility Events

### A1. Named patterns in the academic literature

Standard directional rotation predicts opposite-sign taker flow on the two legs of a paired binary during a repricing event: informed buyers should accumulate the newly-favored leg while exiting or shorting the disfavored leg, producing positive net flow on one and negative on the other. What we observe — positive net flow on both legs simultaneously — does not fit this rotation framing. The closest academic analogues come from three distinct sub-literatures.

**Cross-asset informed trading and ETF arbitrage.** Ben-David, Franzoni, and Moussawi (2018, "Do ETFs Increase Volatility?", *Journal of Finance*, https://doi.org/10.1111/jofi.12727) document that ETF arbitrage activity propagates shocks from the ETF level to underlying securities by generating simultaneous trading pressure across the basket. When an ETF dislocates from its net asset value, arbitrageurs simultaneously buy (or sell) the ETF and take offsetting positions in the components, but the aggregate effect on component order flow need not be purely directional: the speed and simultaneity of arbitrage execution creates trading-volume increases on all legs of the basket, not a clean rotation. By analogy to ETF-arb literature, where informed flow appears on multiple components simultaneously during a repricing event, a prediction-market paired-binary may show bilateral taker pressure when a sharp participant is uncertain which leg the market has mispriced and crosses both spreads to lock exposure — or when the price discovery mechanism requires hitting both books to enforce the no-arb constraint. This is inference by analogy; the ETF literature does not directly test paired-binary markets.

Hasbrouck (1995, "One Security, Many Markets: Determining the Contributions to Price Discovery," *Journal of Finance*, Vol. 50(4), 1175-1199, https://www.bauer.uh.edu/rsusmel/phd/hasbrouck95.pdf) defines information share as the proportional contribution of each venue's innovations to the efficient price. For a paired binary, the "efficient price" is shared: when DIA's YES moves 10¢ down, MED's YES must move ~10¢ up by the no-arb constraint (bid_A + bid_B ≈ 98¢). If information arrives that shifts the shared probability, there is no uniquely-correct venue in which the adjustment should appear; rational traders will cross whichever book offers the best price for their direction, potentially hitting both books if execution is fragmented across time. The information-share framework thus predicts neither pure rotation nor pure bilateral flow; the direction depends on where liquidity sits and how the no-arb constraint propagates. The operator's empirical observation of bilateral-positive taker flow is consistent with a scenario where the sharp participant bought YES-DIA (expecting the price to recover after the repricing) and bought YES-MED (expecting MED to continue rising) simultaneously — i.e., the "rotation" happened in the underlying probability but the taker-flow sign did not rotate because both legs were at prices the informed participant found attractive after incorporating the news.

**Two-sided taker pressure during news arrival.** Patell and Wolfson (1984, "The Intraday Speed of Adjustment of Stock Prices to Earnings and Dividend Announcements," *Journal of Accounting and Economics*, https://www.gsb.stanford.edu/faculty-research/publications/intraday-speed-adjustment-stock-prices-earnings-dividend-announcements) demonstrate that earnings releases produce immediate spikes in return variance and trading volume, persisting for several hours, without necessarily producing clean directional imbalance in the first minutes — both buyers and sellers respond to the new information simultaneously, each having different priors. Lee (1992, "Earnings News and Small Traders: An Intraday Analysis," *Journal of Accounting and Economics*, 15(2-3), 265-302, https://econpapers.repec.org/article/eeejaecon/v_3a15_3ay_3a1992_3ai_3a2-3_3ap_3a265-302.htm) finds that small traders trade on both sides of earnings surprises during the announcement window, with the net imbalance emerging only afterward as positions are resolved. The implication: in the first minutes after information arrival, both-sides taker pressure reflects heterogeneous prior updating, not pure directional informed trading. For prediction markets where the "information event" is pre-event settlement proximity (the match is about to start), similar heterogeneous updating dynamics would predict transient bilateral-positive taker flow while the market finds the new equilibrium.

**VPIN and multi-directional toxicity.** Easley, López de Prado, and O'Hara (2012, "Flow Toxicity and Liquidity in a High-Frequency World," *Review of Financial Studies*, 25(5), 1457-1493, https://academic.oup.com/rfs/article-lookup/doi/10.1093/rfs/hhs053) define VPIN as a scalar toxicity metric based on signed volume imbalance within equal-volume time buckets. VPIN is by construction asymmetric: it measures the *imbalance* between buy-initiated and sell-initiated taker flow, so a market with high bilateral taker flow (large volumes on both sides) will have *low* VPIN if the imbalance is small, even during a volatile period. This is precisely why VPIN does not natively flag the bilateral-flow pattern seen in the KXATPMATCH-25AUG20DIAMED burst: large volumes on both legs with moderate imbalance will produce a VPIN reading closer to zero than to one, yet the book is undergoing regime change. The operator needs a complement to VPIN that flags *total* taker volume, not just its direction — a "total taker pressure" metric rather than a "net taker imbalance" metric. Building on the Round 3 synthesis (`plex_premarket_execution_round3_2026-05-21.md`) which established VPIN conceptually, this round notes that the specific bilateral-positive signature requires a different detector construction (see A3 below).

**Prediction-market and sports-betting specifics.** Croxson and Reade (2011, "Exchange vs. Dealers: A High-Frequency Analysis of In-Play Betting Prices," https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/08863923c027e92da81bc24a867de767f01cf661) find that Betfair in-play prices incorporate information within seconds during matches. Their focus is on closing-line accuracy and information leadership, not on taker-flow directionality. The paper most relevant to taker-flow mechanics at prediction-market-like venues is Becker (2025, "The Microstructure of Wealth Transfer in Prediction Markets," https://jbecker.dev/research/prediction-market-microstructure), analyzing 72.1 million Kalshi trades: the paper finds that liquidity makers earn positive excess returns while takers lose, consistent with informed-taker flow arising primarily in single-leg markets without systematic bilateral documentation. Whelan (2025, "Makers and Takers: The Economics of the Kalshi Prediction Market," MPRA Paper 126350, https://ideas.repec.org/p/pra/mprapa/126350.html) documents favorite-longshot bias and confirms that Kalshi's market structure is quote-driven, with a small number of well-informed makers. Neither paper directly examines bilateral taker flow during pre-event burst windows; the bilateral-positive pattern documented in `KXATPMATCH-25AUG20DIAMED` appears to be an empirical finding without a direct published precedent in prediction-market-specific microstructure literature.

Two recent prediction-market microstructure papers provide methodological context. Dubach (2026, "The Anatomy of a Decentralized Prediction Market," arXiv:2604.24366, https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.24366) — 30 billion events over 52 days, 600 Polymarket markets — finds that trade-direction estimation from public LOB feeds agrees with on-chain ground truth on only ~59% of buckets (vs ~80% Lee-Ready accuracy on Nasdaq). This confirms that the operator's `taker_flow_in_minute` series should be validated against on-chain settlement records where possible; feed-derived sign estimates are noisy (F33 depth-chain gap applies: large prints may traverse multiple levels that the tape records as one event). Gómez-Cram et al. (2026, "Prediction Market Accuracy: Crowd Wisdom or Informed Traders?," LBS/Yale, https://www.crowdfundinsider.com/2026/04/275859-elite-traders-dominate-polymarket-research-reveals-3-of-accounts-steer-most-price-discovery/) find that ~3% of Polymarket accounts drive the majority of price discovery and respond faster to new information; their trades predict next-period price changes at statistically significant levels. This supports reading the bilateral taker burst as informed flow, but whether informed participants hit both books simultaneously versus sequentially is not directly tested in that paper.

### A2. The bilateral-flow pattern: synthesis and framing

The literature does not have a single named pattern for "both legs of a paired binary simultaneously net-positive taker bought." The closest named concepts are:

- **Simultaneous arbitrage execution** (ETF arb literature): bilateral flow across basket legs forced by arbitrage mechanics, where the direction of each leg's flow is determined by which side offers the better price, not by directional conviction.
- **Two-sided price discovery window** (Patell-Wolfson, Lee 1992): during the initial minutes of information arrival, both buyers and sellers are active as heterogeneous priors update, producing elevated volume on both sides before imbalance emerges.
- **Informed bracket execution** (by analogy to event-driven equities): a participant who knows the match is about to start, and knows that both current prices are stale, may cross *both* spreads as a form of liquidity consumption that establishes exposure while market impact is still manageable. The resulting pattern — net bought on both legs — reflects execution strategy rather than directional conviction on a single outcome.

For the B23 (paired-leg bilateral mechanism) doctrine code: the bilateral-flow pattern is consistent with B23's framing that a paired-binary market's no-arb constraint creates coupling between the two books such that a single informed event produces correlated trading pressure across both legs. Literature analogizes most directly to ETF arbitrage (bilateral forced by constraint) and less directly to equity event windows (bilateral from heterogeneous updating).

The A39 (cents-vs-ROI asymmetry) code is relevant here: if the bilateral-flow signature functions as a pull-trigger for resting bids, the cost of a miss (staying in the book and getting adversely filled) must be weighed against the cost of a false positive (pulling and missing a good entry). The literature on signal-aware execution (Cartea, Jaimungal, and Bank-Körber 2024, "Optimal Execution and Speculation with Trade Signals," arXiv:2306.00621, https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.00621) formalizes this as a stopping-rule problem where the agent conditions on a signal intensity process; the bilateral-flow indicator is a candidate signal for that intensity.

### A3. Real-time detection signal framework

**Threshold calibration.** Define "bilateral taker pressure" (BTP) within equal-volume time buckets across both legs (analogous to VPIN's equal-volume bucket approach): the trigger fires when signed taker flow exceeds a positive threshold on *both* legs simultaneously. Calibrate thresholds using rolling quantiles of the quiet-window distribution (T-4h through T-90m). In the KXATPMATCH event, the quiet window showed near-zero taker flow, making the signal-to-noise during the burst essentially infinite — corpus-wide calibration will establish the correct quantile.

**Window length.** The B25 doctrine code (minute-cadence simulator overstatement, 2.4× factor) is directly relevant: if the bilateral event resolves sub-minute, a minute-cadence detector registers it one minute late. This latency is acceptable for a bid-pull trigger (pull before the second burst minute, not the first); it is unacceptable for a real-time taker trigger.

**Conditional gating.** Following Cartea-Jaimungal (2013, HMM regime gating, https://doi.org/10.1080/1350486X.2013.771515), the bilateral-flow detector should be gated by: (1) time-to-event active window (T-90m to T-20m); (2) paired_arb_gap_maker above baseline (confirming stale book); (3) BBO velocity already nonzero on either leg (bilateral-flow becomes a confirmation, not a leading signal, when the book has already started moving).

## Sub-question B: BBO Consumption Velocity as Discontinuity Indicator

### B1. The literature on continuous vs. discontinuous price processes

Most LOB models, including the foundational Cont, Stoikov, and Talreja (2010, "A Stochastic Model for Order Book Dynamics," *Operations Research*, 58(3), 549-563, https://pubsonline.informs.org/doi/10.1287/opre.1090.0780) model, assume that the best bid and ask evolve continuously as a result of order arrivals and cancellations following Poisson or related processes. The model's implicit assumption is that the book is consumed gradually: takers walk the ladder level-by-level, and the BBO moves in small steps as each level clears. The KXATPMATCH-25AUG20DIAMED observation directly violates this assumption: the book sits at *exactly* zero velocity for 2.5 hours, then jumps ±30-43¢ in single minutes. This is not a continuous-walk regime; it is a regime-switching process with a near-absorbing state (zero velocity, zero volume) and an episodic burst state.

**Jump detection in price and quote dynamics.** The canonical machinery for detecting discontinuities in high-frequency price processes is the bipower variation framework of Barndorff-Nielsen and Shephard (2004, "Power and Bipower Variation with Stochastic Volatility and Jumps," *Journal of Financial Econometrics*, https://academic.oup.com/jfec/article-lookup/doi/10.1093/jjfinec/nbh001; and 2006, "Econometrics of Testing for Jumps in Financial Economics Using Bipower Variation," *Journal of Financial Econometrics*, https://academic.oup.com/jfec/article-lookup/doi/10.1093/jjfinec/nbi022). Bipower variation, computed as the sum of products of adjacent absolute returns, converges to integrated variance under continuous dynamics but diverges from realized variance when jumps are present. The ratio test (realized variance / bipower variation) detects jump components. Lee and Mykland (2008, jump detection via standardized returns at high frequency) extend this to event-level detection, identifying specific minutes containing jumps rather than characterizing the distribution of jump intensity.

By analogy to jump-detection literature, the BBO velocity series `bid_consumption_velocity` and `ask_consumption_velocity` (signed minute-deltas of BBO prices) can be tested for the presence of jumps using an equivalent bipower-variation ratio on the velocity time series. The key difference from the canonical equity-price application: (a) the series has a near-zero baseline during the quiet phase (the denominator of the ratio test is essentially zero), which makes the standardized test statistic pathological without modification; (b) the series is sparse (most minutes have zero velocity), so the standard asymptotics require adjustment. The operator's empirical observation of "flat at exactly zero for 2.5h, then ±30-43¢" is a jump signature that no continuous-process model would generate under its null hypothesis.

**Regime-switching LOB models.** Huang, Lehalle, and Rosenbaum (2015, "Simulating and Analyzing Order Book Data: The Queue-Reactive Model," *Journal of the American Statistical Association*, https://doi.org/10.1080/01621459.2014.982278 and arXiv:1312.0563) introduce a key structural insight: the order book dynamics should be split into periods during which the reference price (typically mid-price) is constant, and transition events between those periods. Within each constant-reference-price period, the LOB evolves as a Markov queuing system with state-dependent intensities; the switch to a new reference price level is a separate stochastic mechanism. This architecture directly accommodates the KXATPMATCH pattern: the 2.5-hour quiet phase is a constant-reference-price period (both legs' BBOs sit fixed), and the T-65m burst is a sequence of rapid transitions to new reference-price levels. The Queue-Reactive model does not assume continuous drift; it assumes discrete level switches separated by continuous within-level queuing dynamics. This is arguably the best-fitting model class from the literature for the observed pattern, though it was developed for liquid markets (Bund futures) and its calibration to thin, episodic binary markets has not been directly demonstrated.

Jaimungal and Cartea (2013, cited above) employ an HMM to identify slow/active regime states in tick data; regime-switches are detected by changes in trade intensity and quote dynamics. The B25 overstatement factor (2.4×) is specifically a burst-regime problem: in the quiet state the continuous-fill assumption produces near-zero fills correctly, but once the burst begins the simulator applies the same IID arrival-rate assumptions while real fills cluster sub-minute in a Hawkes cascade.

Lalor and Swishchuk (2025, *Quantitative Finance*, https://doi.org/10.1080/14697688.2025.2541007) find that LOB data exhibit non-Markovian duration properties; the 2.5-hour near-absorbing quiet state followed by an explosive burst is precisely the structure semi-Markov models capture (sojourn in the quiet state is heavy-tailed, conditioned on match start proximity).

**When the continuous-fill assumption fails.** The assumption is appropriate when participants arrive continuously, the LOB has multi-level depth, and no discrete exogenous event triggers coordinated repricing. It fails for the operator's context on all three dimensions: participation is clustered near T-60m to T-20m (B23); LOB depth at the BBO is thin — often 1-10 contracts — so a single taker clears the entire level discontinuously (F33: the gap to the next level may be 5-10¢, forcing a BBO jump when the level clears); and the match start time is a discrete, known exogenous event that triggers coordinated repricing.

Bergault and Cognéville (2024, arXiv:2410.06839, https://arxiv.org/html/2410.06839v1) develop LOB models for sparse order books in intraday electricity markets using an inhomogeneous Poisson process conditioned on book state. No direct equivalent exists for Kalshi binary markets, but the electricity paper's conclusion — that standard LOB models fail when significant gaps exist between order levels — maps precisely to the F33 concern.

### B2. Hawkes processes and temporal burstiness

Bacry, Mastromatteo, and Muzy (2015, "Hawkes Processes in Finance," *Market Microstructure and Liquidity*, https://doi.org/10.1142/S2382626615500057) show that mutually-exciting Hawkes processes naturally produce temporal burstiness: an initial taker event self-excites subsequent arrivals in a cascade, then decays to baseline. The Hawkes intensity collapsing to near-zero after a burst — and remaining there until the next exogenous shock — matches the zero-velocity quiet phase exactly.

Filimonov and Sornette (2012, "Quantifying Reflexivity in Financial Markets," *Physical Review E*, https://doi.org/10.1103/PhysRevE.85.056108) operationalize the Hawkes branching ratio as a measure of endogeneity. Near criticality (branching ratio → 1), a small exogenous shock triggers a self-sustaining cascade. By analogy to Filimonov-Sornette, the approach to T-0 (match start) is a predictable transition toward transient criticality. The `trade_clustering_in_minute` feature (Round 3) is a Hawkes-intensity proxy that should spike at burst onset. The G22 doctrine code (three-axis deployment math) grounds this: Hawkes-excitation onset is the trigger for bid-pulling, before the burst's adverse volume peaks.

### B3. Paired binary simultaneity versus lag

A key empirical question is whether BBO discontinuity events (large velocity jumps) appear simultaneously on both legs or sequentially. The literature on cross-venue price leadership is informative. Hasbrouck (1995, cited above) establishes that price discovery leadership can be measured via information shares and that the "lead" market shows the innovation first. For paired binaries under a no-arb constraint, theory predicts *simultaneous* propagation: if a participant updates the DIA leg, the no-arb constraint immediately makes the MED leg also mispriced (since DIA_bid + MED_bid must ≈ 98¢), so the MED repricing should follow within the same clock interval as the DIA repricing, or even be triggered by the same participant's simultaneous execution across both books (B23 bilateral mechanism).

In the KXATPMATCH v2 chart, the velocity pattern on DIA (bid −19¢, ask −21¢ summed) and MED (bid +23¢, ask +21¢ summed) are described as occurring during the same burst window; the per-minute resolution of the tape does not resolve sub-minute sequencing. The Hayashi-Yoshida (2005) estimator for asynchronous high-frequency correlation would allow testing simultaneous vs. lagged co-movement if the data had sub-minute timestamps. At minute cadence, the question of simultaneous vs. lagged cannot be answered from this corpus at this resolution — but the no-arb theoretical prior strongly favors simultaneity within the same minute, consistent with the chart.

**Detector composition implication.** If the two legs update simultaneously, a single detector watching both velocity series (using an AND condition: both jump in the same minute, in the same direction relative to the no-arb constraint) is more powerful than treating them as separate detectors. A single bilateral BBO-velocity jump event is cleaner evidence of an informed repricing than a unilateral jump on one leg, because unilateral jumps can arise from noise (one-leg book depletion without information) while bilateral jumps are unlikely to occur simultaneously by accident in a thin market.

### B4. Feature set for the discontinuity detector

Building on Round 3's `trade_clustering_in_minute` and `ask_consumption_velocity` features, the discontinuity detector should incorporate:

| Feature | Literature source | Role |
|---|---|---|
| `bid_consumption_velocity` + `ask_consumption_velocity` (own) | Cont-Stoikov-Talreja 2010; v2 Panel 5 | Raw jump signal; same-direction bid/ask jump = book pulled |
| `bid_consumption_velocity` + `ask_consumption_velocity` (partner) | B23 bilateral mechanism | Confirms paired repricing |
| `trade_clustering_in_minute` (both legs) | Round 3; Bacry-Mastromatteo-Muzy 2015 | Hawkes self-excitation proxy |
| `paired_arb_gap_maker` spike | v2 Panel 4 | Confirms book pulled, not gradually consumed |
| Realized velocity variance (rolling 3-min) | Andersen-Bollerslev-Diebold-Labys realized vol analogy | Flags zero-variance → burst-variance regime transition |

The key diagnostic: **"book pulled"** versus **"book gradually consumed."** In the pulled case, `bid_velocity` and `ask_velocity` on the same leg jump in the same direction simultaneously (both down for DIA, both up for MED — exactly as observed), and `paired_arb_gap_maker` spikes transiently. In the gradually-consumed case, ask_velocity spikes before bid_velocity (takers exhaust the ask side; the bid adjusts after), and distortion stays flat. The F33 code grounds this: with no resting depth between the current BBO and the next level 30¢ away, a gradual path is physically impossible.

Hashbrouck and Saar (2009, "Technology and Liquidity Provision," SSRN 994369, https://ssrn.com/abstract=994369) document fleeting orders — quotes posted and cancelled within milliseconds. At minute cadence, the velocity series collapses the distinction between fleeting repricing cycles and genuine taker walk-through. This reinforces B25: the detector identifies the burst correctly at minute resolution, but sub-minute resolution is required to attribute mechanism.

## Joint-Pattern Synthesis: One Composite Event or Two Separate Signals?

### The empirical conjunction

For the one observed event, all four signatures — bilateral-positive taker flow, BBO velocity discontinuity, distortion blowout, and volume burst — occur in the same ~T-65m to T-30m window. The question is whether this conjunction is a coincidence (four separate phenomena that happen to co-occur in thin markets near event time) or a single underlying event-type that manifests across all four measurement dimensions simultaneously.

### What the literature suggests

The literature does not have a direct answer for prediction-market paired binaries, but it provides strong structural support for the composite-event interpretation.

**Information events are composite by construction.** Easley and O'Hara's information-event framework (foundational work across multiple papers, including the 1992 *Journal of Finance* paper on information and the bid-ask spread, and the VPIN series) treats an "information event" as something that simultaneously: (a) triggers informed taker flow, (b) causes market makers to widen quotes or pull liquidity, (c) increases volume, and (d) increases price volatility. All four of the operator's signatures are predicted outcomes of a single "information event" under the Easley-O'Hara framework. The ETF arb literature (Ben-David et al.) adds that cross-asset arbitrage enforces coupling between correlated books, producing simultaneous bilateral effects — consistent with both legs showing the same burst pattern.

**Hawkes processes link burstiness, volume, and price dynamics.** Bacry-Mastromatteo-Muzy (2015) and Filimonov-Sornette (2012) both argue that in self-exciting order-flow models, the arrival of an exogenous shock (the information event) triggers a cascade of endogenous events that simultaneously drives up volume, taker pressure, and price volatility. The bilateral-flow signature and the BBO velocity discontinuity are both expressions of the same Hawkes cascade: the taker crosses the spread (bilateral flow), the passive liquidity providers pull and repost (BBO velocity jump), and the aggregate of these repricing events produces the distortion spike and volume burst. Under this interpretation, there is one underlying event (a critical Hawkes cascade triggered by an exogenous event), not four separate phenomena.

**Regime-switching models treat the transition as unitary.** The Queue-Reactive model (Huang-Lehalle-Rosenbaum 2015) explicitly defines "events" as transitions between reference-price levels, not as individual trades or quote updates. Under this framing, the T-65m burst is a single "level-transition event" that produces all four observable signatures as its outputs. The bilateral flow, the velocity jump, the distortion, and the volume are all facets of the same underlying event type: a transition from the quiet reference-price state to the burst state.

### The architectural recommendation

The literature strongly supports treating the four signatures as **facets of one composite event-type** rather than four independent signals with separate thresholds and response policies. The architectural implication:

**Single composite detector, not four separate detectors.** The composite event can be defined as: "at least two of four simultaneous conditions are met within the same 1-2 minute window — (1) bilateral-positive taker flow above quiet-phase quantile threshold on both legs, (2) BBO velocity |Δ| above threshold on either leg with same-direction bid/ask jump, (3) distortion above threshold, (4) volume above quiet-phase quantile threshold." Any two of these four conditions firing simultaneously is stronger evidence of the composite event than any one condition firing alone, because spurious individual signals are possible (a large single-leg taker without bilateral context, a book reprice without volume, etc.) but spurious co-firing of two or more is much less likely in thin markets.

**Signal hierarchy within the composite.** Under the Hawkes-process framing, the temporal ordering within the burst is: (1) volume onset (first taker events arrive); (2) bilateral taker pressure (multiple takers cross both books); (3) BBO velocity jump (passive providers pull and repost); (4) distortion spike (gap between pulled bid and still-resting ask on the partner leg). This ordering suggests that volume onset is the leading indicator and distortion spike is the lagging indicator within the composite event. The BBO velocity jump and bilateral taker flow are contemporaneous (same minute). A bid-pull trigger that fires on the BBO velocity jump or bilateral flow condition — rather than waiting for the distortion spike — will fire earlier in the sequence, when the composite event is still in progress and the book is most adverse.

**Corpus-wide test prior.** The joint-pattern hypothesis predicts that in a corpus-wide analysis of premarket_tape_v1, all four signatures will be statistically associated with each other (not independent), and their co-occurrence will be a stronger predictor of adverse fill probability than any individual signal. The prior from the Easley-O'Hara information-event framework, the Hawkes cascade model, and the Queue-Reactive level-transition model all independently predict this result. The n=1 observation is consistent with this prior, but the corpus test is necessary to assign effect sizes and false-positive rates.

**Why the signals might remain partially separate.** The composite-event interpretation could be wrong if: (a) thin-market noise produces frequent unilateral BBO velocity jumps or bilateral flow spikes without the other components (common for low-liquidity markets with market-maker quote refreshes); or (b) the distortion spike occasionally appears without volume or taker flow (a passive market-maker pulling quotes without an information trigger). The G19 code (candle null patterns — OI is null for 2025-Aug events) means the distortion component cannot be validated for the current corpus on the OI dimension; only the bid/ask and taker-flow dimensions are available. This is a partial measurement limitation that the corpus-wide test must account for.
