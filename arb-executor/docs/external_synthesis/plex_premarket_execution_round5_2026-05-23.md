## Sub-Q A — Is the Favorite-Drift Gradient Consistent with Literature?

### A1. Magnitude calibration

The Scope A T4 gradient registers approximately ±10–11¢ of mid-drift across the full T-4h to T-20m window for the extreme anchor bands (r05_14 and r85_94), tapering monotonically to near-zero at the 45–54¢ coin-flip band. The first comparison question is whether this magnitude is anomalous, expected, or small relative to what the sports-betting microstructure literature has measured.

The most apposite benchmark comes from the tennis-specific pre-match price formation study by an SSRN working paper (Scribd rendering of SSRN 5043714, 2024) that analyzes longitudinal price series across multiple bookmakers from open to close. That paper estimates an average learning rate of µγ ≈ 0.049 across the premarket window, with favorites showing a significantly higher learning rate (0.061) than longshots (0.022). Critically, "larger price changes from the open to the close are associated with higher winning rates" — confirming that the drift toward favorites is information-driven, not noise. The paper's finding that opening prices are often noisy and biased, becoming more accurate as kickoff approaches, directly supports the T4 pattern as a structural convergence phenomenon rather than an artifact. For a favorite priced at 85¢ at T-4h, a +10¢ drift to the T-20m anchor is geometrically consistent with a learning-rate-driven trajectory over a ~220-minute window.

Levitt (2004) establishes the North American sportsbook context: for NFL spreads, the posted line changes only 1.4 times on average across five days, and 90% of lines end within one point of where they opened. This describes a very different market structure — bookmaker-held, not exchange-traded — but the underlying mechanism (bookmakers deliberately shade lines toward favorites because bettors systematically overbid favorites) is the demand-side mirror of the exchange-side drift observed in the T4 corpus. Where Levitt's bookmakers extract surplus by keeping lines slightly too low for favorites (making underdogs the net-positive side), exchange markets under LMSR-adjacent pricing or AMM seed pricing will show the same correction path but realized dynamically through actual trading: early prices seed too low for favorites and too high for underdogs, with the book correcting through T-20m.

The [Whelan et al. (2026)](https://www.karlwhelan.com/Papers/Kalshi.pdf) study of 300,000+ Kalshi contracts directly confirms that Kalshi exhibits a systematic favorite-longshot bias: contracts above 50¢ earn small positive returns while contracts below 20¢ lose over 60% of their value. Their regression evidence shows intercepts negative and slopes positive, consistent with the T4 gradient's shape (underdogs drift down, favorites drift up, coin-flip approximately static). The Becker (2026) microstructure study of 72.1 million Kalshi trades similarly finds the longshot bias to be the dominant pricing pattern, with contracts at 5¢ winning only 4.18% of the time (implying −16¢ of mispricing) and contracts at 95¢ winning at 95.83%. These are end-state (settlement) mispricing numbers, not pre-match trajectory numbers, but the direction and approximate magnitude of the terminal bias is consistent with a ±10–11¢ premarket convergence toward efficiency from a seeded starting point.

**Verdict on magnitude:** The ±11¢ terminal drift at the extremes is consistent with documented Kalshi structural bias in the literature. It is *larger* than what Levitt-style NFL endpoint studies imply for American football spreads (where line movement is minimal), but tennis is a different market: two-outcome binary exchange, thinner books, shorter betting windows for some legs, and an AMM/maker-seed pricing mechanism at Kalshi that likely produces larger seed-to-equilibrium paths than a Pinnacle-style sharp-driven opening. The magnitude is in the right range and consistent with structural origin.

### A2. Four-category invariance

The near-identical gradient across ATP_MAIN, WTA_MAIN, ATP_CHALL, and WTA_CHALL (with WTA_CHALL showing modestly larger extremes on small N) is the most striking pattern in the T4 table. Two explanations compete.

**Structural / supply-side explanation.** The Kalshi AMM seed pricing assigns initial implied probabilities to paired contracts independently of which tour is being priced. If seed prices reflect base-rate match statistics or simple Elo estimates that systematically underweight heavy favorites and overweight heavy underdogs — a standard consequence of using bounded LMSR parameters — then the convergence gradient would be proportional to the seed-to-equilibrium distance, not to the informational richness of the specific market. The four-category invariance is consistent with this: the same seed-overstatement mechanism operates identically whether the market is an ATP_MAIN marquee or a WTA_CHALL low-liquidity event, because the seed is set by the same algorithm.

**Demand-side / behavioral explanation.** The Cortis and Newall (2021) literature review confirms that in two-outcome markets with one outcome having probability > 0.5 — exactly the binary YES/NO structure on Kalshi — sports bettors exhibit *favorite bias* (overbetting favorites, driving prices too high) rather than longshot bias. This is structurally opposite to horse racing where multiple outcomes produce longshot bias. For a Kalshi tennis binary, if retail bettors overbid favorites and overbid underdogs (buying YES on both sides due to the YES/NO asymmetry Becker documents), the book corrects toward a DISCOUNT-for-favorites, PREMIUM-for-underdogs steady state — exactly the T4 starting point at T-4h, with drift completing the convergence by T-20m. Four-category invariance in this explanation reflects the universality of the underlying retail behavioral bias rather than circuit-specific information dynamics.

The Cain, Law, and Peel (1996) work on bookmaker-implied favorite-longshot bias in fixed-odds markets, and Snowberg and Wolfers' (2010) NBER paper differentiating risk-love from misperceptions as the bias source, lean toward misperceptions (probability weighting in the sense of Kahneman-Tversky) as the dominant mechanism. Under misperception, the bias operates uniformly across market quality — explaining the four-category invariance as a robustness check that the driving force is a generalized pricing artifact, not circuit-specific information. By analogy to Snowberg-Wolfers, inference (not direct evidence on Kalshi): the invariance is most consistent with the misperception / seed-artifact channel rather than tour-specific informational content.

### A3. Trajectory shape

Most closing-line efficiency literature measures endpoints (opening vs. closing or vs. settlement). Literature directly on the sub-hourly *shape* of pre-match trajectories is sparse. The key exception is the SSRN 5043714 tennis paper, which finds: (a) the learning process "emerges not immediately at the beginning of the betting period but increases toward the middle, continuing almost until closing"; (b) the slope coefficients show a *pause* in learning between the 12th and 30th percentile of the betting period, then resume; (c) the RMSE continuously declines. This describes a non-monotonic speed trajectory — slow early, accelerating mid-window, peaking near close — even though the cumulative price path is directionally monotonic.

The Sugawara-Mori (2025) Ornstein-Uhlenbeck model for horse racing betting shows that herding and informed-bettor interactions produce mean-reverting macroscopic dynamics (odds converging toward fair value), with the convergence speed increasing as bettors resolve uncertainty closer to post time. By analogy: the T4 flat-to-start, steep-at-close shape is consistent with an OU-type process where the drift rate is proportional to the gap between current price and fair value, with uncertainty about fair value declining non-monotonically as information accrues.

**Practical implication for the bid-laying policy.** A bid placed at T-4h at a substantial discount below current mid is not guaranteed to fill early: the trajectory shape literature suggests low drift velocity in the first 60–90 minutes, with acceleration as T-0 approaches. For the operator's patient-on-underdogs thesis, this means a bid placed at T-4h at an intermediate target may sit unfilled for 100+ minutes before the drift accelerates into it — consistent with patience being structurally justified, not merely preference.

## Sub-Q B — Is the Asymmetric Policy Thesis Sound?

### B1. Informed-money direction question: does sharp money favor favorites or underdogs?

This is the most important literature challenge to the operator's thesis. The thesis assumes the bot wants to *own favorites at discount entries* — i.e., it wants to align with the direction that sharp-informed flow is eventually going to push prices. But if sharp/informed money systematically *fades* favorites, the thesis is loading into adverse selection.

Levitt (2004) directly addresses this. His central finding is that bookmakers deliberately shade prices so that favorites attract more bets but *win less than half the time at the shaded spread*. The optimal bookmaker strategy, given systematic bettor bias toward favorites, is to move the line *against* favorites (make favorites slightly too expensive), not toward them. The implication: if bettors overbid favorites relative to the efficient price, then the bookmakers and, by extension, informed arbitrageurs (who take the other side of the favorable bet) are *fading favorites* — consistent with what Avery and Chevalier (1999) describe as the sentimental-bettor pump: sentimental bettors push favorites up, creating a systematic edge for counter-bettors who fade the sentiment. Levitt's Table shows that home favorites and visiting favorites both attract disproportionate share of wagers (56% and 68%), yet cover the spread less than 50% of the time.

This looks like a challenge to the thesis. But the critical distinction is between *sportsbook* favorite-fade (the bookmaker-world phenomenon) and *exchange favorite-drift* (the Kalshi phenomenon). On an exchange, there is no bookmaker to shade prices. Kalshi's favorite-longshot bias, as documented by Whelan et al. (2026) and Becker (2026), runs the other direction from what a sharp-is-fading-favorites mechanism would predict: on Kalshi, favorites *earn* small positive returns and underdogs *lose* heavily. This is structurally opposite to the Levitt bookmaker world. The reason: on Kalshi's exchange, the correction mechanism is *demand-side liquidity provision* — makers who provide liquidity on favorites (buying YES at discount) are the "sharps" capturing the bias, and they earn positive returns. The drift from T-4h to T-20m represents this correction process playing out: early-placed maker bids at discount are the alpha-capture mechanism, not the adverse-selection mechanism.

The Tetlock (2008) prediction market paper provides a direct cross-venue anchor: on TradeSports, limit order traders (makers) who passively execute during *non-informative* periods earn positive returns; it is only during informative periods (when new information arrives and prices should jump discontinuously) that limit orders become adversely selected. The pre-match drift window, absent late-breaking injury news, is not a continuously-informative period — it is a slow correction process where limit order placement is the appropriate strategy. The B23 (paired-leg bilateral mechanism) doctrine code is relevant here: the paired-binary constraint means that buying YES on a favorite simultaneously sells YES on its counterpart, keeping the book approximately arb-free, which reduces the adverse-selection risk of a resting bid.

**The sharp-money direction on exchange markets is structurally WITH favorites (alpha = maker bid on discounted favorite), not against them.** The Levitt fade-the-favorite pattern is a sportsbook-specific pricing artifact that does not transfer to exchange mechanics.

### B2. The maker-then-cross hybrid execution framework

The operator's hybrid design — bid passively at discount, cross aggressively if the bid is not filled within time X or if drift moves price past the bid — is a well-recognized execution pattern in the financial microstructure literature under the name "passive-then-aggressive" or "liquidity-seeking with stop." Glantz and Kissell (*Algorithmic Trading Methods*, 2003) and Almgren and Chriss (1999, [SSRN 53501](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=53501)) provide the foundational variance-cost tradeoff: for a given execution deadline, the optimal strategy trades off the variance of the incomplete position (opportunity cost of not filling) against the cost of market impact (paying the spread to cross). The Almgren-Chriss efficient frontier shows that as deadline approaches, the optimal strategy *front-loads* execution — becoming more aggressive as time-to-deadline shrinks — which is exactly the operator's cross-fallback logic.

The most directly relevant formalization is Cartea, Bank, and Körber (2024, *Optimal Execution and Speculation with Trade Signals*, [arXiv:2306.00621](https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.00621)). Their framework models an agent with access to a signal process — precisely the T4 drift heuristic — and derives that the optimal strategy is to trade passively (post limit orders) when the signal indicates favorable near-term price movement, and to trade aggressively (cross into the taker zone) when the signal indicates that the favorable window is closing or when the position deadline approaches. The structure of the operator's thesis maps onto their framework: the drift signal is the alpha signal, the T-20m anchor is the deadline, and the cross-if-not-filled rule is the terminal aggression action.

The *implicit option* in the passive-then-aggress pattern deserves explicit treatment. The operator's waiting period is equivalent to holding a *free option to fill at discount*: so long as the bid is resting and the price has not moved past the bid, the bot can fill at the maker price (better than the T-20m taker floor). If the price moves away (drift up for favorites), the option expires worthless and the bot crosses into taker — giving back the spread but preserving the directional position. Brogaard, Hendershott, and Riordan (2019, [SSRN 2655927](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=2655927)) find that limit orders submitted by informed participants contribute significantly to price discovery — *not because they fill*, but because they reveal information and anchor price discovery. By analogy: the bot's resting maker bid serves as an anchor that signals willingness to hold a favorable position, with the cross-aggress fallback ensuring the position is established even when the maker bid goes unfilled.

**The hybrid try-passive-then-aggress design is sound for the asymmetric direction the thesis requires.** For favorites (drift runs away from a low bid), the cross-fallback converts a possible miss into a guaranteed entry. For underdogs (drift runs toward the bid), the patient-wait design matches the drift mechanics. The asymmetry is not architecturally arbitrary — it is derived from the different geometric relationship between bid placement and drift direction in each regime.

**One literature-grounded refinement:** the cross-fallback for favorites should condition on drift *velocity*, not just drift *occurrence*. Cartea-Jaimungal (2013, [DOI:10.1080/1350486X.2013.771515](https://doi.org/10.1080/1350486X.2013.771515)) use an HMM to detect slow/active regimes: when the book is in a burst state (high BBO velocity, per Round 4's composite detector), paying the spread to cross is justified because the drift is accelerating and each additional minute of delay costs more than the spread. When the book is in a slow state, continued patience is warranted even if the passive bid has not filled, because the drift is slow enough that the next minute's mid is unlikely to jump past the bid.

## Sub-Q C — Where Does FV Anchor Fit in the Policy?

### C1. Kalshi-as-follower hypothesis

The T5 data shows Kalshi last-traded sitting +0.9¢ Main / +1.1¢ Challenger *above* cross-book consensus (where consensus is derived from offshore sportsbooks via the betexplorer feed). This positive premium is small but systematic across tens of thousands of FV-available minutes. The Hasbrouck (1995, [DOI:10.1111/j.1540-6261.1995.tb04054.x](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/j.1540-6261.1995.tb04054.x)) information share framework provides the natural interpretation tool: if Kalshi *leads* price discovery, it should price ahead of consensus; if it *follows*, consensus prices in ahead and Kalshi reverts to it. The positive premium (Kalshi above consensus) is consistent with Kalshi being a *lagging* venue that systematically prices favorites slightly too high before correcting toward the sharp offshore consensus — i.e., Kalshi is a follower.

The Gonzalo-Granger (1995) component share decomposition and the Yan-Zivot (2010) information leadership share provide more granular tests. By analogy to the India NSE/SGX Nifty study (Sundararajan and Balasubramanian 2023, applying Hasbrouck and Gonzalo-Granger) where offshore futures *lead* the onshore spot market, the prediction here is that Pinnacle (offshore, sharp liquidity) leads Kalshi (US-regulated, retail-dominated) in tennis price discovery. The systematic +0.9–1.1¢ Kalshi premium over consensus is the structural sign of this follower relationship: Kalshi prices embed an ongoing retail-demand premium that arbitrageurs have not fully competed away, because US-only retail access limits the arbitrage bandwidth.

Additional structural reasons for the Kalshi follower premium: (a) Kalshi fees create a transaction-cost wedge that prevents perfect cross-book arbitrage; (b) Kalshi's US-only regulatory structure means the sharp cross-venue arb community has limited access compared to Pinnacle's global clientele; (c) the settlement currency risk (all US-dollar settlement vs. offshore multi-currency exposure) creates a small but real premium for US-based bettors who prefer Kalshi's guaranteed settlement.

**Practical policy implication (doctrine A39):** When FV consensus exists and Kalshi is priced *above* it (positive fv_delta), this confirms the follower hypothesis — Kalshi is running hot, and the convergence trade is to sell Kalshi (or equivalently, buy YES on the underdog who will drift toward consensus). When fv_delta is negative (Kalshi below consensus), Kalshi is anomalously cheap versus the sharp market — buy YES on the favorite aggressively because the convergence trade is aligned with the drift.

### C2. Signal composition: conditional gate vs. additive signal vs. multiplicative weight

The three options for composing the T4 drift heuristic with the FV signal have different theoretical properties.

| Composition mode | What it assumes | When it is correct |
|---|---|---|
| **Conditional gate** (use FV when available; if FV disagrees, downsize or abstain) | FV is a *binary override* — agreement with drift is necessary for full position | Correct when FV is a sufficient statistic for whether drift will complete |
| **Additive signal** (weighted sum of drift-implied alpha + fv_delta) | FV and drift are independent contributors to expected PnL | Correct when FV and drift have orthogonal information content |
| **Multiplicative confidence weight** (scale position size by degree of FV agreement) | FV scales confidence in the drift thesis, not the drift direction itself | Correct when FV agreement reduces variance of drift outcome |

The Cartea-Jaimungal (2023) trade-signals framework and the Lehalle-Neuman (2018) signal-incorporation paper both model signals as *drift predictors* in a stochastic price process. Their results show that when two signals are positively correlated (both point in the same direction), they contribute to the conditional drift estimate in approximately additive fashion — but the marginal contribution of the second signal diminishes when the first is already strong. This suggests **multiplicative confidence weighting** is the closest theoretical optimum: FV agreement scales the position size upward (more confidence in the drift thesis), FV disagreement scales it downward, and FV absence leaves the base drift-implied position unchanged.

The conditional-gate design is easier to implement and approximately equivalent to multiplicative weighting at extreme agreement/disagreement values. Given FV coverage is only 3.5% of minutes, the gate design loses negligible expected value vs. the multiplicative design while being substantially easier to reason about. However, the gate design should be directional: *only apply the gate when FV disagrees with drift; when FV agrees, allow the full position*. This is strictly superior to a symmetric gate that abstains on both agreement and disagreement.

### C3. Operating without FV — robustness at 96.5% coverage gap

The FV layer is absent for 96.5% of corpus minutes. The policy must be robust at this coverage level. The mixed-frequency econometrics literature (Ghysels' MIDAS regression framework) provides the theoretical grounding for operating with an auxiliary signal that arrives at a lower frequency than the primary signal: the recommendation is to weight the low-frequency signal at the last available observation and decay the weight as time since observation grows. For FV, this means: use the last FV reading for up to X minutes into the premarket window, then decay its weight to zero as time passes without a new reading.

More practically: the T4 drift heuristic is strong enough on its own (±11¢ monotonic across all four categories, consistent at the corpus level) that FV's primary role is as a *confidence enhancer*, not a *necessary condition*. Tetlock (2008) finds that limit order makers in prediction markets who trade during non-informative periods (not during news events) earn positive expected returns even without cross-venue signal. The UNKNOWN state in the fv_state_strategy_v1 spec — which defaults to standard V4 behavior — is the appropriate degradation: at 96.5% coverage, the drift heuristic is the primary signal and FV is the enhancement.

The one exception: WTA_CHALL. Zero FV coverage means the drift signal is entirely unanchored to cross-book consensus. The WTA_CHALL T4 extremes (−12.60¢ and +13.08¢) rest on small N (887 legs), and the absence of any FV anchor means outlier events (late injury news, market-thinness artifacts) are undetectable. The policy should apply a higher skepticism discount to WTA_CHALL extreme-band positions in the absence of any FV signal — not because the drift is directionally wrong, but because the extreme values may reflect thin-book noise more than in the ATP_MAIN corpus.

## Sub-Q D — Failure Mode Ranking

Three named failure modes, ranked by literature-grounded severity from highest to lowest:

### D1. **Failure Mode 1 (Highest): Favorite-drift reversal from late exogenous information**

This is the highest-severity failure mode because it produces correlated losses: a position filled on a favorite that then becomes an underdog due to late news (injury, withdrawal, match condition change) loses the full entry price plus the convergence premium. Avery and Chevalier (1999) show that pre-match line movement is driven by sentimental and informed traders in different directions — the key insight is that when *informed flow* reverses direction, the price can move *against* the resting bid by more than the drift would have added.

The Croxson-Reade (2014) Betfair analysis of goal arrivals in soccer demonstrates that prices on Betfair incorporate information within seconds of news arrival — the in-play speed of adjustment. While this is in-play literature, the implication for pre-match is that when injury news hits any venue, prices reprice discontinuously (as seen in Round 4's burst characterization). The forward-looking detection signal suggested in Round 4 — bilateral taker flow, BBO velocity, distortion spike — correctly identifies this failure mode *after it begins*; the question is whether cross-venue lead-lag (Pinnacle moves before Kalshi) provides an *earlier warning*.

**Detection signals for reversal:**
- **Cross-venue sentinel (strongest):** Monitor Pinnacle/offshore consensus for the same match. If consensus mid moves *opposite* to the T4 drift direction by ≥3¢ in any 5-minute window, treat as a potential reversal signal. The Hasbrouck follower hypothesis predicts Kalshi will lag Pinnacle by minutes; cross-venue monitoring provides several minutes of warning before the Kalshi book reprices.
- **Round 4 composite detector (secondary):** Two-of-four conditions (bilateral taker flow, BBO velocity, distortion spike, volume burst) firing in the direction *opposite to the expected drift* is the primary same-venue reversal signal.
- **Time-of-day injection points (inference, not evidence):** The period T-2h to T-90m is when training reports and warm-up observations are typically available; this is the highest-risk window for exogenous late news to reverse a resting favorite bid.

**Corpus-testable proxy:** Compare T4 mid-drift at events where the match actually started (confirming both players were healthy) against the full corpus. If the gradient is stable, reversal events are outliers, not a structural drag. If the gradient is driven by a mix of normal convergence and reversal events, the sample N is already large enough to estimate the reversal frequency.

### D2. **Failure Mode 2 (Medium): Lumpy/burst drift — the bid-level skip**

The Round 4 synthesis characterized composite burst events where markets reprice ±30–43¢ in single minutes (the KXATPMATCH-25AUG20DIAMED case). If a favorite's full ±11¢ drift happens in a single burst at T-65m rather than smoothly over 220 minutes, a bid placed at T-4h may sit waiting while the burst skips over the bid price entirely (the market jumps from 75¢ to 85¢ without pausing at the 77–79¢ bid level).

Bacry, Mastromatteo, and Muzy (2015, [arXiv:1502.04592](https://arxiv.org/abs/1502.04592)) show that Hawkes process intensity cascades produce exactly this pattern: an initial exogenous shock self-excites a burst of subsequent trades, repricing in a cascade rather than via smooth arrival. Rambaldi, Filimonov, and Lillo (2018, [Phys. Rev. E 97, 032318](https://link.aps.org/doi/10.1103/PhysRevE.97.032318)) show that intensity bursts can be detected in real time using Hawkes-based clustering statistics; the initial time of a burst can be determined with precision comparable to the typical inter-event time. The T2 data (trade-print availability: 26–46% of minutes, with a mild U/W shape showing higher activity at extreme bands) suggests that burst events, when they occur, will be detectable as an anomalous density spike in the trade-print series.

**Severity calibration:** Medium, not high, because burst drift is not inherently adverse: a burst that skips past the bid means the bot *doesn't* fill at the discounted maker price — it either crosses at the new, higher taker price (net effect: atlas-baseline entry, no maker improvement) or misses the entry entirely. The worst-case outcome is the atlas-baseline taker entry, which is the conservative floor. The failure mode is not a loss; it is a *foregone gain*. This is lower severity than reversal (which produces an outright loss).

**Detection and response:** The Round 4 composite detector (volume burst → bilateral flow → BBO velocity → distortion spike) fires during burst events. For the burst-drift failure mode, the response is the same cross-fallback already in the policy: when BBO velocity exceeds a threshold and the bid has not filled, switch to aggressive taker. The key calibration question (corpus-testable, not a literature question) is the *time window* between burst onset and bid-skip — i.e., how quickly does the burst price clear past the bid level, and is there a 1–3 minute window to cross before the new price stabilizes at a level that neutralizes the entry advantage.

**Calendar regularity (inference):** By analogy to Pinnacle's practice of releasing sharper limits as a match approaches (referenced in the Levitt context as "daily-limit-raise times"), bursts may concentrate at specific time-windows before match start (T-2h, T-90m, T-60m) as institutional participants receive sharper pricing authority. This is inference, not direct evidence from the Kalshi corpus; the T2 per-minute distribution from Scope A is the corpus-testable proxy.

### D3. **Failure Mode 3 (Lower): Spread pathology at crossing moment**

The Scope A T1 data shows p90 spreads fanning out toward T-20m: ATP_MAIN 2→5¢, WTA_MAIN 3→7¢. This means a non-trivial minority of crossing moments will have spreads of 5–7¢ rather than the 1–2¢ median. The cross-fallback assumes the spread is payable; at 5–7¢, paying the full spread to cross into taker may exceed the expected remaining drift residual, making the cross net-negative.

Cartea and Jaimungal (2013) formalize spread-aware execution: the cost of taking liquidity is the half-spread plus fees, and the optimal aggression threshold depends on whether the expected continuation value of waiting (further drift residual) exceeds the immediate crossing cost. The Almgren-Chriss framework with spread costs (the "temporary market impact" formulation) provides the threshold condition:

*Cross if: expected remaining drift > (spread / 2) + taker_fee*

For a favorite at r85_94 with ~11¢ total drift and, say, 7¢ already completed, the remaining residual is ~4¢. If the spread is 6¢ at T-25m, crossing costs 3¢ (half-spread) plus fees — making the net gain of crossing ~1¢ or near-zero. The policy should gate the cross-fallback with a real-time spread condition.

**Severity is lower than failure modes 1 and 2** because: (a) spread pathology does not produce an outright loss — a blocked cross simply leaves the passive bid open, hoping for a late fill; (b) the p90 spread fanning out is a tail condition (10% of minutes), not a typical condition; (c) the cross-fallback is not required — the policy already tolerates passive-only fills. The failure mode is a degraded execution quality, not an adverse fill.

**Detection and conditional rule:** Monitor real-time `spread_close` at the moment the cross-fallback would trigger. If spread > *max_cross_spread* (corpus-calibrated threshold, likely 3–4¢ for ATP_MAIN, 4–5¢ for WTA_MAIN), do not cross — revert to passive-only mode for that event. The doctrine A39 (cents-vs-ROI asymmetry) is directly relevant: paying 6¢ to lock an expected 1¢ net gain is not rational. In the corpus, spread pathology correlates with the distortion `paired_arb_gap_maker` (doctrine F33): when distortion is large, the book is thin and spread is wide — so the distortion signal from T3 can proxy for imminent spread pathology.

## Synthesis Closer

The strongest version of the operator's thesis is this: **the T4 gradient is a structural convergence phenomenon, not a trading artifact, and maker-bid placement from T-4h captures a portion of that convergence as maker-improvement alpha above the atlas's established T-20m taker baseline.** The literature supports this in four ways:

1. The direction of Kalshi's favorite-longshot bias (Whelan et al. 2026, Becker 2026) confirms that favorites systematically earn positive returns at settlement — meaning the drift direction is correct, and buying favorites at a discount is the positive-EV side.

2. The asymmetric policy (aggressive-on-favorites, patient-on-underdogs) maps correctly onto the asymmetric geometry of bid placement vs. drift direction. The hybrid maker-then-cross framework is theoretically grounded in the Cartea-Bank-Körber (2024) signal-informed execution model and is the appropriate resolution of the variance-vs-cost tradeoff when a drift signal exists and a deadline is present.

3. The Kalshi-as-follower hypothesis (supported by the positive fv_delta and the Hasbrouck information-share framework) means cross-book FV consensus from Pinnacle is an *earlier signal* of where Kalshi is going to be, not a competing estimate — making FV a valid confidence enhancer rather than a noisy override.

4. The four-category invariance of the T4 gradient strengthens the inference that the driving mechanism is structural (AMM seed-artifact + retail behavioral bias) rather than circuit-specific, making the policy portable across categories without separate calibration.

**The most exposed failure mode is Failure Mode 1 (drift reversal from late exogenous news)**, not spread pathology or burst skip. The mitigant is cross-venue monitoring of the sharp offshore consensus as a leading indicator of adverse reversal, triggering bid cancellation before the Kalshi book reprices. The Round 4 composite detector (volume burst + bilateral flow in the *wrong* direction) is the fallback within-venue signal, but cross-venue is earlier. If the corpus supports it, building a test for sustained contra-directional movement in Pinnacle consensus as a bid-kill condition is the highest-value addition to the policy.

The most tractable corpus-testable proxy for the reversal failure mode is: isolate T4 by whether the match actually started as scheduled vs. was walkover/retirement. If the gradient holds for non-walkover matches and collapses or reverses for walkovers/retirements, the reversal risk is concentrated in an event type with its own observable signal (official withdrawal announcements) that appears on Betfair in-play before Kalshi reprices.
